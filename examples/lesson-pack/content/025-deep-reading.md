# Deep reading: choosing the useful model

## A model answers a particular question

Chemical models are selective descriptions. They make some features of a reaction easier to track by leaving other features in the background. In a Brønsted–Lowry account, the central event is the transfer of a proton. The learner asks which species loses that proton, which species gains it, and how the products relate to the reactants. That account is especially useful when the task is to identify conjugate pairs or follow proton transfer through an equilibrium.

The Lewis account directs attention elsewhere. It asks where an electron pair begins, where that pair is accepted, and which bond is formed as a result. A proton-transfer reaction can be described this way because the accepting base supplies an electron pair to the proton. The same language also applies to reactions in which no proton moves. The models overlap, but they do not foreground the same evidence.

Choosing between them is therefore not a contest for the one permanently correct label. It is a decision about explanatory purpose. If a prompt asks for conjugate pairs, proton bookkeeping is the economical route. If it asks how a coordinate bond forms, tracking the electron pair is more revealing. A strong answer names the model and states what observable change that model is being used to explain.

## Conjugate pairs are relationships, not isolated labels

The words *acid* and *base* can sound like permanent identities, but Brønsted–Lowry roles are assigned within a particular reaction direction. A species is called an acid when it donates a proton in the represented process and a base when it accepts one. The product left after donation is the conjugate base; the product formed after acceptance is the conjugate acid. Each conjugate pair therefore differs by one proton and, correspondingly, by one unit of charge.

This relational view prevents a common error: labelling every negatively charged species as a base and every positively charged species as an acid without comparing reactants and products. Charge is useful evidence, but it is not sufficient by itself. A learner should place the before-and-after formulas beside one another, identify the one-proton difference, and only then assign the roles. The comparison supplies a check that a memorized label cannot.

Reaction direction also matters. When an equilibrium is read in reverse, the donating and accepting species change. The two members of each conjugate pair do not change, but which member is acting as the acid or base in that direction does. This is why a complete explanation attaches every role to the represented transfer instead of listing labels without a mechanism.

## Broader does not mean more informative

Because the Lewis definition includes proton-transfer cases and also covers electron-pair acceptance without proton transfer, it is often described as broader. Broader scope is useful, but it does not make the broader model the best answer to every question. A map of an entire country contains more territory than a street map; the street map may still be the better tool for finding a particular doorway. Models should be judged against the work they are being asked to do.

Suppose a learner must identify the conjugate acid of ammonia after proton acceptance. The Brønsted–Lowry model names the tracked particle and makes the ammonia/ammonium relationship explicit. Rephrasing the same event only as electron-pair donation is compatible with the chemistry, but it hides the one-proton comparison the task requires. Conversely, forcing proton-transfer language onto the formation of a Lewis adduct without a transferable proton would omit the relevant electron-pair interaction.

This principle extends beyond acid–base chemistry. Scientific explanations are bounded by scale, assumptions, and purpose. More general language can unify cases, while narrower language can expose a relation that matters for prediction or calculation. Expert model choice is not merely recalling definitions; it is matching a representation to the evidence and the requested conclusion.

## Translate symbols into a causal sentence

Chemical equations compress many claims into a small space. A learner can manipulate the symbols correctly while remaining unable to explain what changed. To test understanding, translate the equation into a causal sentence: identify the donor, identify the acceptor, name the transferred proton or electron pair, and connect each product to its precursor. Then reverse the translation by checking that the verbal account predicts the displayed formulas and charges.

For a proton-transfer equation, one useful sequence is: compare species across the arrow; locate the one-proton difference; pair each reactant with its conjugate product; assign donor and acceptor roles; and check the charge change. For a Lewis interaction, locate an available electron pair, identify an electron-pair acceptor, and state which new bond or interaction the pair accounts for. Neither sequence replaces structural reasoning, solvent effects, equilibrium, or quantitative treatment. It simply makes the selected model explicit enough to inspect.

The final sentence should also state a limit. A donor–acceptor label does not by itself determine reaction extent, acid strength, rate, or biological consequence. Those questions require additional evidence and often another model. Stating the limit is part of using a model well: it marks the boundary between what the representation explains and what has not yet been established.
