# When a number agrees with itself—and is still wrong

A laboratory checks a temperature probe five times in a stable reference bath. The display reads 2.0, 2.1, 2.0, 2.0, and 2.1 °C. The readings huddle together, so the probe seems dependable. Then the laboratory discovers that the reference value was 0.0 °C.

The probe answered the same way each time. It also answered the wrong question about temperature by about two degrees.

That is why “Is this measure good?” is too blunt. A useful measurement audit asks four narrower questions:

1. **Target:** What quantity or idea is the study trying to represent?
2. **Operation:** What exactly produces the recorded value?
3. **Consistency and error:** What happens when the operation is repeated, and how do results compare with a defensible reference?
4. **Meaning:** What interpretation will researchers place on the value, and what evidence supports that interpretation?

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to use those questions to critique an operational definition, separate random from systematic measurement error, distinguish repeatability from reproducibility, and judge whether evidence supports an intended construct interpretation.

## Retrieve the comparison underneath the measure

Measurement sits inside a study design. Before continuing, try this low-stakes check from the prerequisite lesson.

:::{check}
:id: check-prerequisite-variables
:kind: diagnostic

A team compares two fictional sample-storage methods. The team assigns storage method A or B, holds sample volume and storage time alike, and records enzyme activity with one instrument.

Name the independent variable, dependent variable, and two controlled variables. Then identify the part of the sentence that still needs a measurement audit.
:::

The independent variable is storage method. The dependent variable is the recorded enzyme activity. Sample volume and storage time are controlled variables. The remaining audit concerns how the instrument turns “enzyme activity” into a value, how much that value varies, and what the value can justifiably mean.

## What this lesson does not claim

This is a college-intro audit, not a full course in psychometrics or measurement uncertainty. You will not calculate reliability coefficients, design a clinical diagnostic instrument, or build a complete uncertainty budget. You will learn which evidence question to ask and which conclusions the available evidence cannot support.
