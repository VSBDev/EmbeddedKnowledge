# Defend the measurement judgment

The four-question audit is now one continuous argument:

1. Name the target quantity or construct and the intended use.
2. Specify the operation that produces the record.
3. Test the relevant error sources under named repeatability or reproducibility conditions and, where appropriate, against a defensible reference and calibration chain.
4. Decide whether the accumulated evidence supports the intended interpretation in the stated population and setting.

No single result can stand in for the whole chain. An explicit operation can be noisy. A repeatable instrument can have a stable offset. A calibrated device can feed a poor construct indicator. A plausible construct indicator can still behave differently across populations or settings.

## Mastery check

The assessment contains four untimed, retakeable items worth 14 points:

- `item-scatter-shift`: interpret repeated reference measurements;
- `item-operational-construct`: critique and repair an operational definition;
- `item-conditions-calibration`: distinguish repeatability, reproducibility, and reference evidence; and
- `item-transfer-greenhouse`: defend a complete judgment in a new biological-imaging context.

Attempt every item before opening its answer and reasoning. Mastery means at least 11 of 14 points, including at least 2 points on `item-operational-construct` and at least 3 points on `item-transfer-greenhouse`. Those minimums prevent a strong error calculation from hiding a weak construct-validity judgment.

On a retry, use the alternate context named in each item's feedback rather than replaying the same answer sequence. Explain what changed in your reasoning.

## Recovery route

If you miss the scatter/shift item, return to the two-instrument table in the previous scene. Calculate a mean and range, then write separate sentences for dispersion and reference difference.

If repeatability and reproducibility blur together, list every condition that stayed fixed and every condition that changed. Do not choose the label until the list is visible.

If calibration sounds like “the device is now correct,” return to the four-question audit. Calibration establishes a relation under specified conditions; correction, adjustment, fitness for purpose, and construct meaning remain separate questions.

If the operational-definition or transfer item is weak, write four headings—target, operation, consistency/error, meaning—and put one claim and one missing piece of evidence under each. Then retry with the alternate case.

## Accessibility and alternatives

Every essential relation in the concept diagram is repeated in ordered prose. Every quantitative table can be read row by row, and its relevant mean, range, and direction are stated in words after the learner's attempt. No distinction depends on color, position, sound, motion, hover, dragging, speed, or a physical procedure.

Responses may be typed, dictated into the learner's own assistive technology, or organized as prose, headings, or a table. The assessment is untimed and retakeable. All people, instruments, organizations, and data are fictional, and no personal, patient, workplace, message-log, or health information is required.

## Keep the boundary

This lesson supports evaluation of measurement claims in bounded scientific examples. It does not validate a real clinical instrument, establish diagnostic or treatment guidance, certify laboratory competence, or replace field-specific standards and expert review.
