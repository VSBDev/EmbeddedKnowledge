# Ask what had to happen before a result became visible

A result can be numerically precise and still answer the wrong version of a question.

Imagine a study of a seed coating. The report says that 80% of coated seedlings and 70% of uncoated seedlings looked healthy after 30 days. The percentages seem easy to compare. Then you learn that the researchers calculated them only among seedlings still alive on day 30. Ten coated seedlings and 30 uncoated seedlings had died and disappeared from the denominators. The arithmetic is correct, but the reported comparison is not the planned question, “What fraction of all assigned seedlings are alive and healthy on day 30?”

That gap is the practical heart of bias detection: reconstruct the path from the target question to the observations that were allowed into the result. At each step, ask what was excluded, mismeasured, lost, or left unseen—and whether that process was related to the comparison or outcome.

In this lesson, **bias** means a systematic distortion of a study estimate or conclusion. It is not an accusation that a researcher acted dishonestly, and it is not the same as ordinary sampling fluctuation. A larger sample can make an estimate more precise while leaving a systematic distortion in place.

## What you will be able to do

By the end, you will be able to:

- detect selection, information, observer, attrition, publication, and survivorship bias from the mechanism described in a study;
- explain what part of the evidence path is distorted and state a direction only when the available facts support one; and
- propose a check or design safeguard aimed at the stage where the distortion arises.

The focus is detecting threats to validity. Detailed causal diagrams, mathematical bias correction, and meta-analysis calculations are outside this lesson. The cases are fictional teaching cases; they make no clinical or agricultural recommendation.

## Check the two tools this lesson assumes

Answer before reading the feedback.

1. A university draws 200 names at random from all enrolled students. In a different study, 200 volunteers are assigned at random to two study conditions. Which action selects a sample, and which creates comparison groups?
2. A scale gives almost the same reading every time but is always 3 kg too high. Is the repeated measurement reliable, valid, both, or neither for measuring true mass?
3. Why might an outcome assessor be kept unaware of which intervention a sample received?

### Feedback

1. Drawing names selects a sample from a target population. Random assignment creates comparison groups after units enter the study. One does not substitute for the other.
2. The scale is repeatable, so it is reliable in this narrow sense, but the consistent offset threatens measurement validity.
3. Keeping the assessor unaware of group assignment removes one route by which that knowledge could influence a judgment or classification.

## Recovery route

If either distinction felt uncertain, keep these two questions beside you:

- **Selection question:** Who or what did the investigators want to describe, and by what rule did an observed unit enter the comparison?
- **Measurement question:** What property did the investigators intend to measure, and could the measurement rule behave differently across the groups being compared?

Return to the three diagnostic questions after the next scene. If you can explain why the accurate but consistently offset scale is not rescued by repeated readings, you have enough measurement background to continue. If you can explain why random assignment says nothing about how representative the volunteers are, you have enough sampling background to continue.
