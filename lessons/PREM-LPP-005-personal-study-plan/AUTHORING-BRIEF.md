## Observable objectives and evidence

1. Construct a weekly capacity boundary that records fixed commitments, usable study blocks, an accessibility-equivalent route, and protected recovery capacity. Evidence: assessment item `item-build-week`.
2. Allocate concept learning, problems, supervised lab work or lab preparation/debrief, delayed review, and recovery in a dependency-aware sequence. Evidence: `item-repair-overfilled-plan` and `item-build-week`.
3. Revise a later week from error-log and completion evidence without turning every missed task into a growing backlog. Evidence: `item-adapt-week` and `item-transfer-plan`.

Every objective is instructed, modelled, practiced with fading support, assessed, and paired with remediation.

## Anchor task and explanatory model

Anchor question: “How can a weekly plan remain academically complete when time, energy, access, and deadlines are constrained?”

The target explanatory model is an author-created four-pass planning procedure:

1. **Bound capacity:** begin with realistic usable blocks and fixed commitments, not an unlimited task list.
2. **Place dependencies and fixed work:** schedule prerequisite concept learning before dependent problems and place supervised or deadline-bound labs first.
3. **Distribute learning functions:** create repeated contacts for acquisition, retrieval/review, problem solving, and lab preparation/debrief rather than treating one long exposure as all functions.
4. **Reserve and revise:** leave explicit recovery capacity, then use completion and error-log evidence to protect, move, split, replace, or remove work at the next weekly review.

The procedure is a transparent instructional synthesis of the Premed course contract and learning-content standard. It is not asserted to be a uniquely optimal timetable or a clinical recommendation.

## Relations, assumptions, limits, and exclusions

- Capacity constrains what can be scheduled; therefore planning starts by bounding available study opportunities.
- Dependencies constrain order; therefore prerequisite explanation precedes dependent problem or lab application.
- Learning functions differ; therefore one block labelled “study” does not automatically provide concept construction, retrieval, problem solving, or investigation evidence.
- Retrieval and delayed review require more than one contact; therefore at least one later contact must be visible in the plan.
- Error-log evidence changes the next action; therefore the plan is revised rather than treated as a fixed promise.
- Recovery capacity is deliberately uncommitted capacity or a reduced route, not a reward that must be earned.

Excluded: prescribing universal daily or weekly hours, sleep or health guidance, diagnosing fatigue or disability, guaranteeing grades or admission, directing unsupervised laboratory work, or claiming that a calendar proves mastery.

## Learner difficulties and discriminating cases

- Boundary case: a visually balanced timetable allocates equal hours to every category but schedules a dependent problem set before concept acquisition. It is not dependency-aware.
- Boundary case: a plan includes “review” only immediately after reading. It lacks a delayed retrieval contact.
- Misconception to repair: filling every usable block appears efficient, but it makes the plan unable to absorb a disruption without stealing future recovery or prerequisite work. The replacement model protects reserve before disruption occurs.
- Boundary case: moving all missed tasks into the next week preserves a task list but creates a backlog, not a sustainable route. The replacement decision is protect/move/split/replace/remove, based on outcome and error evidence.

## Worked example, fading, transfer, and retrieval

- Complete worked example: a fictional learner has twelve self-defined focus blocks, one fixed supervised lab, and evidence of a representation-translation error. The solution schedules ten study blocks, protects two reserve blocks, orders concept work before a related problem set, separates lab preparation from debrief, and places delayed retrieval later in the week.
- Completion problem: repair a partially filled week using a provided sequence checklist.
- Supported problem: choose which of two candidate placements preserves prerequisites and recovery.
- Independent problem: construct a week from a compact evidence record.
- Mixed selection: decide whether a disrupted task should be protected, moved, split, replaced, or removed.
- Transfer: adapt the model for a week with intermittent connectivity and a fixed supervised practical, preserving the learning operations through offline text routes.
- Delayed retrieval: at the next weekly review, reconstruct the four passes before opening the template; in later domain lessons, use error-log evidence to schedule a delayed contact with an earlier node.

## Mastery, feedback, remediation, and retry

Mastery requires a feasible capacity boundary, all five outcome functions represented, dependency-aware order, at least one delayed retrieval contact, protected recovery capacity, and a revision rule tied to evidence. Feedback states what is present or missing, names the governing planning relation, and prescribes the next action. Retakes use a new fictional constraint/evidence set so the learner must reconstruct the procedure.

Remediation is targeted:

- missing capacity boundary: redo the constraint ledger only;
- category list without sequencing: annotate prerequisites and fixed events;
- no delayed contact: place a later retrieval block and state what it retrieves;
- no recovery capacity: remove, split, or defer lower-priority work before adding reserve;
- backlog carryover: classify each missed task with protect/move/split/replace/remove and justify it from the mapped outcome or error evidence.
