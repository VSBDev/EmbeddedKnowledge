# Build the four-pass planning model

A weekly plan is a constraint-and-evidence model. It represents what is fixed, what can move, what each activity is meant to produce, and what happens if the first route becomes unavailable.

## Pass 1: bound capacity

Start with a **capacity boundary**: the study opportunities that are realistically usable after fixed commitments and access constraints are represented. A **study block** is a learner-defined planning unit, not a universal duration. One learner may use equal blocks; another may label them short, standard, and extended. The plan only requires that the labels are consistent enough to prevent double-booking.

Record four kinds of constraint:

| Constraint | Question | Neutral record |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Fixed commitment | What cannot move inside this week? | “Fixed commitment A” |
| Access | When are equipment, supervision, connectivity, or a quiet text route available? | “Online access available” |
| Usable study capacity | Which opportunities can genuinely hold a learning task? | “Ten usable blocks” |
| Recovery capacity | Which capacity remains uncommitted or reduced? | “Two reserve blocks” |

Do not write medical, disability, employment, or caregiving details. The plan needs the constraint, not its private cause.

## Pass 2: place dependencies and fixed work

A **fixed commitment** is time- or access-bound. Place institutionally supervised labs and true deadlines first. Then annotate dependencies:

- concept acquisition before a dependent problem or investigation;
- lab preparation before the supervised session;
- lab debrief after the session;
- targeted prerequisite repair before another task that uses the failed relation; and
- an earlier learning episode before its delayed retrieval contact.

Priority is therefore relational. A short prerequisite repair can outrank a larger assignment when the larger task depends on that prerequisite.

## Pass 3: distribute learning functions

A **learning function** is the job an activity performs. “Study biology” hides the job; “retrieve the transport model from memory, then explain one failed prediction” exposes it.

The course distinguishes acquisition, retrieval, problem solving, investigation, application, and reflection/planning. For weekly scheduling, this lesson groups that finer-grained cycle into five planning categories: concept learning covers acquisition, problem solving covers application, lab work covers investigation plus its preparation and debrief, retrieval/review remains distinct, and reflection/planning appears in the revision rule and protected recovery route rather than as a separate sixth block. One block may serve more than one function, but each promised function needs observable evidence. For example, reading a worked solution may support concept construction; it is not automatically evidence of independent problem solving.

Plan a **delayed retrieval contact** after the initial learning episode. The useful interval depends on the retention goal, material, prior knowledge, and retrieval success. The model therefore requires a visible later contact but does not impose one fixed number of days.

## Pass 4: reserve and revise

Protect recovery capacity before disruption occurs. Add a **contingency** for a task whose medium or access could fail. A contingency preserves the learning operation where possible: an offline text explanation and typed graph interpretation can replace an online display, whereas “skip all reasoning” cannot.

At weekly review, classify each unfinished or ineffective task with a **revision decision**:

- **protect** because it is a required outcome, prerequisite, fixed event, or due retrieval;
- **move** because it remains necessary and another feasible slot exists;
- **split** because a smaller coherent part can be completed without losing the target operation;
- **replace** because another task better addresses the same evidence; or
- **remove** because it is redundant, optional, or no longer serves the mapped outcome.

Do not copy every task forward. Use completion evidence, error classes, prerequisite relations, and due retrieval to decide.

## Read the complete ledger

The planning representation is a text ledger:

| Block | Constraint or activity | Learning function | Why now | Evidence | Contingency |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| A | Fixed commitment | Lab | Supervision available | Recorded participation plus later debrief | Institutional rescheduling route |
| B | Example activity | Concept | Prerequisite for C | Explained relation | Offline text version |
| C | Example activity | Problem | Applies B | Independent solution and check | Split into representation and execution |
| R | Uncommitted | Recovery | Absorbs disruption | Not counted as completed study | Reduced route if used |

Read row by row in chronological order. The table does not encode meaning by color or position alone.

## Accessibility and alternatives

Use a calendar, paper, spreadsheet, or plain text. The equivalent text form for each entry is: “When or sequence; activity; function; dependency; evidence; contingency.” No task requires dragging, color, speech, precise pointer movement, continuous connectivity, or a timed response. If a lab is inaccessible, use the institution's approved accommodation or alternative; this lesson does not invent a substitute and does not claim physical skill from a non-physical route.
