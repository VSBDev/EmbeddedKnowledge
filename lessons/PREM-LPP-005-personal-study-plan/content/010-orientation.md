# Plan for a week that can change

A study plan is useful only when it can answer a concrete question: **what will you do, in what order, with the capacity you actually have, and what will change when evidence or access changes?** A list of intentions cannot answer that question by itself.

In this lesson, a sustainable plan means a plan that is internally feasible, includes the learning functions required by the course, protects some recovery capacity, and has a rule for revision. “Sustainable” is not a judgment about effort or character, and this lesson does not prescribe a universal number of hours.

You will build a week that makes five functions visible:

1. concept learning;
2. problem solving;
3. institutionally supervised lab work, or lab preparation and debrief when a lab is scheduled;
4. retrieval and review, including a later contact with earlier learning; and
5. recovery capacity or a reduced re-entry route.

The week will also use evidence from the prerequisite error log. If an error identifies an insecure prerequisite, the plan protects a targeted repair before dependent work. If evidence shows a task is secure or optional, the plan can reduce or remove redundant work.

The four-pass model used here is:

1. bound capacity;
2. place dependencies and fixed work;
3. distribute learning functions; and
4. reserve and revise.

This is an instructional model for the assigned curriculum outcome, not a uniquely optimal productivity system.

## Scope and exclusions

This lesson teaches schedule reasoning. It does not diagnose fatigue, disability, illness, or difficulty; provide health or sleep advice; guarantee grades or admission; override institutional deadlines; or authorize unsupervised laboratory activity. Use generic labels such as “fixed commitment” or “low-access block” instead of recording private details.

Recovery capacity is capacity deliberately left uncommitted or assigned to a reduced route. It is part of the plan, not a reward earned after every other box is checked.
