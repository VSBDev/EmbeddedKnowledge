# Taking the four questions somewhere else

Everything so far has been a health or fitness study with participants, an intervention, and a measured person. None of that is essential. The four questions are about the structure of a comparison, so they apply wherever there is one — which is what makes them worth carrying.

Here the thing being studied is not a person at all. Two warnings before you start. Nobody will tell you what the four questions mean in this setting; working that out is the task. And do not assume the answer is that the study is broken — every case so far has had a fatal flaw, and reading a method section well includes being able to say when a design is sound.

## A study with no patients in it: does the new marking rubric change grades?

> A university writing centre has designed a new marking rubric and wants to know whether essays receive different scores under it. The centre's archive holds every first-year essay submitted over the last three years — 4,000 of them — and all 4,000 will be used. The centre's data officer, who has no contact with the marking team, runs a script that pairs each essay with one of twelve graders and with either the new rubric or the old one, both drawn from a random number generator. The resulting list stays with the data officer, who releases essays one at a time to the office as each grader becomes free. Graders receive the essay text and the name of the rubric they must apply; author names and course codes have been stripped out. Each essay is marked once.

Do the audit before reading on. Work out for each of the four questions what it would even mean here, then judge whether it holds. Write your answer down.

### Working it through

**Entry — what is the sample, and what does it bound?** There is no sample. Every essay in the archive is used, so there is no volunteering, no convenience shortcut, and nobody who declined. This is the first case in this lesson where the entry question comes back clean, and the reason is worth naming: the failures you have been finding — the study-room poster, the eleven clubs that answered an email, the people who clicked a fatigue advertisement — all came from studying whoever was reachable rather than everyone. Take the whole archive and that route closes.

Entry still bounds the answer, just not by bias: the finding is about first-year essays at this university in these three years. Whether it holds for final-year essays, or for another institution's marking culture, this study does not say. A clean entry route makes an answer trustworthy within its address; it does not widen the address.

**Assignment — what is being assigned, and to what?** Here is the real adaptation, and it is easy to miss. Two things are assigned at once: which rubric an essay is marked under, and which of twelve graders marks it. Graders differ in severity, so if the harsher graders happened to receive more new-rubric essays, you would see a rubric difference that is really a grader difference. So the things that must be comparable in expectation are not two piles of essays but two sets of essay-and-grader pairings, and the script assigns both by chance. That is the right design.

It is worth being precise about what this does and does not buy, because it is exactly the point the small breathing trial raised. Randomizing graders makes grader severity balanced *in expectation* across the two rubrics; it does not guarantee that the twelve graders split evenly in this particular run. With 4,000 essays that expectation is a strong guarantee. With 40 it would not have been.

**Foreknowledge — is there an entry decision to corrupt?** No. Apply the test: entry was committed — wholesale, for all 4,000 essays — the moment the study adopted the whole archive, before any assignment existed. No entry decision remained open when the assignments became knowable, so steering is simply not available. If instead someone had been choosing which essays to send onward while able to see the next line of the list, you would be looking at the sports-science failure again, and the randomness of the list would not have helped at all.

The design is also strong on the separate question of the record: the assignments exist in advance as a file, held by someone with no contact with the graders and released essay by essay, so they are documented and cannot be quietly regenerated when someone dislikes one.

**Awareness — who knows, after assignment, and does it reach the outcome?** This is where the interesting answer lives, and it is not "they were careless."

The grader must be told which rubric to apply, because applying the rubric *is* the intervention. There is no version of this study in which the assessor is unaware of the assigned condition. That is the same situation as the physiotherapy trial and the exercise programme: unblindable by the nature of the thing being tested, not through an oversight.

What the team did with the part they could control is exactly right. Author names and course codes are irrelevant to marking an essay and would have supplied graders with expectations about the writer, so they were stripped. When a party cannot be blinded to the intervention, removing every *other* source of expectation is the available protection, and this design took it.

A real weakness remains, and it cannot be fixed by blinding anybody. The outcome is a mark, which is a judgement, and it is produced by someone who knows which rubric they are using. A grader who finds the new rubric clearer, or resents it, can mark systematically differently under it, and no amount of anonymizing the essay touches that. So the repair has to come from somewhere other than the awareness question: mark a subset of essays twice, once under each rubric by different graders, so that the rubric difference can be measured within the same essay and compared against the same grader's behaviour under both. Notice what has happened — the four questions located the problem precisely, and then told you that the answer lies outside them.

**Verdict.** This is a well-built study. Entry is complete, assignment is random over both of the things that needed randomizing, and entry was committed before any assignment could be known, with the sequence documented and separately held besides. The strongest warranted claim is correspondingly strong for a change: for first-year essays in this archive, any difference in average mark between the two rubrics reflects the rubrics rather than who was studied or who was steered into which group. The material boundary is that graders necessarily knew which rubric they were applying, so a difference in how graders behave under an unfamiliar rubric is not separable from a difference in what the rubrics measure — and that limit is a property of the question, not a flaw in the execution.

Notice what transferred and what did not. The four questions transferred intact, and so did the concealment test. What changed was what fills them: "who enrols" became "is anyone deciding which essays get in at all," "the groups" became essay-and-grader pairings, and "who is blinded" became "what is the grader told besides the rubric." What also changed is the shape of the conclusion. A method section is not a search for the flaw; it is a question about which inferences survive, and sometimes most of them do.

## Putting it together

The one-sentence version: **a study's entry route decides who its answer is about, and its allocation, concealment, and blinding decide whether the answer is about the intervention at all.**

Folded down to one line per moment — the full mechanisms are behind you now, so this is the version to carry:

1. **Entry** — a defensible sampling and eligibility route fixes *who the answer describes*. External validity.
2. **Assignment** — random allocation makes the arms comparable in expectation. Internal validity.
3. **The point of allocation** — concealment: entry committed before the assignment is knowable, so nobody can steer who enters. Always arrangeable. Internal validity.
4. **Afterwards** — blinding keeps the made assignment from changing behaviour and measurement. Not always achievable; blind what can be blinded and prefer outcomes that do not rest on judgement. Internal validity.

## Where this connects

Behind you: this lesson stands on the distinction between studies where the researcher assigns the condition and studies where the researcher only observes it, and on confounding. Randomization is the reason an experiment can handle confounders nobody has named — which is precisely what the variables-and-controls lesson could not offer you.

Ahead: selection bias at entry and distorted measurement, both named here, are two members of a larger family. The bias lesson takes that family systematically and adds the ones this lesson deliberately left out — including what happens when participants leave a study unevenly between groups, which is a failure that begins after allocation and that neither concealment nor blinding prevents.

The entry chain also returns in a different guise in the statistics module. There the question is not whether your sample is *biased* but how much an unbiased estimate *bounces around* from one sample to the next, and how that spread narrows as the sample grows. Keep the two apart, because they respond to opposite remedies. A larger sample shrinks the bouncing. It does nothing whatever to the four failures in this lesson, which is why "we should have recruited more people" was the wrong answer every time it came up.

**Come back to this.** A week from now, before you start the bias lesson, do this from memory without reopening the page: write the four questions in time order, and for each one name the safeguard and the specific error it blocks. Then find any study — a news report of a trial, a paper in a course reading, an advertisement citing research — and run the audit on it. The most useful signal is not what the report says but what it declines to say. Reports that do not tell you how allocation was concealed or who was blinded usually have a reason.

## Accessibility and alternatives

Nothing in this lesson has to be seen to be understood, and nothing has to be done at a particular speed.

If you are not viewing the timeline diagram in the opening explanation, you have lost nothing: its long description lists the four moments in order with the safeguard and failure attached to each, and the paragraphs around it make every one of those points in full sentences. The same is true of the table that sets concealment and blinding side by side. Each of its rows is one attribute compared across the two safeguards — what is hidden, from whom, when, what it prevents, whether it is always possible — so reading it as five ordinary comparisons loses nothing, and the paragraph beneath it states the deciding test in words. Where this lesson points at something, it names it rather than telling you where it sits on the page, and no distinction anywhere depends on colour, motion, or hovering.

Every prompt here is a question in words, and any way you answer it counts: writing, typing, dictating, or saying it aloud to yourself. None is timed. None asks you to drag anything or to hear anything.

Every study in this lesson is invented for teaching, so there is nothing to try, take, or measure, and no reason to act on anything described here. You are never asked to share anything about your own health, study habits, or grades. If a described study resembles something you are deciding about in your own life, this lesson is not the place to settle it — it teaches how to weigh evidence, not what to do.

## Recovery route

If the essay audit did not come together, that is a different difficulty from the earlier practice and has a different fix. Try it again after doing one thing first: for each of the four questions, write down who the *person* is in this setting — who decides what gets in, who assigns, who could see ahead, who knows during the marking. The mapping is the difficulty, not the reasoning, and naming the people makes it mechanical.

If you found flaws that were not there, the useful correction is the one this task was built for: check each question for whether the failure it names is actually available in this design, rather than assuming that every study contains one of each.

If the four questions themselves are still unsteady, go back to the recovery list in the practice section, which sorts the repairs by which question is being lost. If assignment and confounding are the shaky ground, the lessons on variables and controls and on choosing a study design are the right place to rebuild before continuing.

Nothing here is a gate, and the assessment that follows can be retaken.
