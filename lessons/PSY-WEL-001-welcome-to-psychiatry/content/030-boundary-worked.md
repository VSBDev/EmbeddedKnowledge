# Worked use: knowledge is not authority

Knowing the vocabulary of a field and being authorized to act within it are different achievements. Use this four-question **scope test** whenever a learning task approaches real clinical action:

1. **Who is affected?** A fictional case, a simulated participant, a population or policy, or a real individual?
2. **What action is proposed?** Explanation and evidence appraisal, or personal assessment and treatment?
3. **What could happen if the conclusion is wrong?** A revisable classroom argument, or a decision affecting someone's health, rights, or care?
4. **What governed route is required?** Ordinary academic study, or supervised clinical practice and jurisdiction-specific authorization?

The test is not a licensing code. Its job is to expose why an action belongs on one side of the learning boundary.

:::{worked-example}
:id: worked-example-noor-boundary

**Task.** Decide which proposed responses to Noor's fictional situation fit this course and explain the decisive reason.

1. Ana maps one question from each lens and states what evidence would help distinguish the possibilities.
2. Ben tells a real roommate with similar difficulties that the roommate has a specific disorder.
3. Chen compares how two fictional university policies could alter sleep opportunity, privacy, access to support, and study participation.
4. Dev recommends that a real classmate stop a medicine.

**Representation.** The known information is limited: Noor is fictional; Ben's roommate and Dev's classmate are real. Ana produces questions. Chen produces a policy analysis. Ben makes a personal diagnostic claim. Dev directs a treatment change. The unknowns include any real person's health information and the local professional rules.

**Principle selected.** Apply the four-question scope test because the difference is not the topic—every learner is discussing mental health—but the affected person, proposed action, possible consequence, and governed route.

**Plan.** Classify each action, justify it with at least two parts of the test, then change one detail to see whether the classification still holds.

**Execution.**

| Learner | Scope decision | Reason |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Ana | Inside academic learning | The case is fictional; the output is a revisable question map, not an assessment or treatment of a person. |
| Ben | Requires clinical authority and adequate evidence | The target is a real person, and the action assigns a diagnosis from a superficial similarity. The possible consequences extend beyond a classroom claim. |
| Chen | Inside academic learning | The objects are fictional policies; the output is a comparison of consequences and evidence needs. It does not decide care for an individual. |
| Dev | Requires clinical authority | The target is a real person, and the action directs a treatment change whose safety cannot be established from the classroom scenario. |

**Independent check.** Change Noor from a fictional student to a real friend. You can still study the general six-lens questions without recording private details or turning the map into an assessment of the friend. You cannot use the lesson to declare what condition the friend has or what treatment the friend should change. The boundary follows the action, not the learner's confidence.

**Interpretation.** Academic learning can become sophisticated: you may compare evidence, analyze systems, reason through fictional cases, and practise in simulation. Those achievements can prepare someone for a separately supervised route, but they do not replace real-patient experience, competency decisions, or legal and professional authorization.

**Self-explanation.** Which part of the scope test—not which mental-health topic—most strongly determines why Ana and Ben fall on different sides of the boundary?
:::

A strong answer names at least the real-versus-fictional target and the proposed action. Ben is not outside scope merely because diagnosis is “advanced.” He is outside scope because he applies a clinical conclusion to a real person without the evidence, supervision, and authority that action requires.
