# Reconstruct the field and your boundary

Close the lesson and try to complete these sentences from memory:

1. The six lenses are…
2. A lens is a question, not…
3. A several-lens account does not assume…
4. The four scope-test questions ask…
5. Academic learning can prepare me to…, but it does not authorize me to…

Now compare your reconstruction with this compact map:

- **Mind, brain, person, relationship, culture, and system** keep six kinds of question available.
- **Select and weigh; do not merely accumulate.** Evidence determines which lenses matter and how much.
- **Knowledge is not authority.** The target, action, possible consequence, and governed route determine the boundary.
- **Stay revisable.** A good account says what evidence could change it.

Return to your opening answer to “What does psychiatry study?” Revise it in one or two sentences. A defensible answer should include the field's medical identity, several levels of study, and a limit on what academic study permits you to do.

## Recovery route

### Mastery check and retry

Complete the four-item assessment attached to this lesson without looking back first. Use its item rubrics after the attempt. Every listed criterion is part of the target; a missed criterion is a study route, not a judgment about you.

- If the six lenses do not come back, return to **Six lenses, six kinds of question**, read the lens questions once, move away from the table, and reconstruct it from memory.
- If you list lenses but cannot weigh them, redo Noor's case using “ask whether” and name one lens that might prove irrelevant.
- If the authority boundary is unclear, apply the four questions to Ana and Ben again, then retry with a different fictional case.
- If policy transfer is weak, replace “consider everything” with one named question, one needed source of evidence, and one limit for each selected lens.

Retake with an equivalent fictional case or policy. Do not memorize the option order.
