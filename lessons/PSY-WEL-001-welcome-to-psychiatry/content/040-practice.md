# Repair shortcuts and practise the map

## A tempting compression

:::{misconception}
:id: misconception-one-real-level

Two shortcuts can sound decisive:

- “Because psychiatry is medical, the brain is the real level; relationships and systems are background.”
- “Because social conditions matter, biology is irrelevant.”

Both fail before the evidence is even considered. Each promotes one lens into a rule that excludes the others. A brain-level account may clarify a process while leaving the person's goals or housing untouched. A system-level account may clarify exposure and access while leaving bodily processes open. The better question is not “Which lens wins?” but “What does this lens explain, what evidence supports it, and what remains unexplained?”

For Noor, night work does not rule out a bodily process. A bodily finding would not make rent, course policy, or Noor's own aims disappear. The lenses can conflict, combine, or prove irrelevant; evidence must decide.
:::

## Completion problem

:::{check}
:id: check-completion-problem

A fictional learner writes: “Noor's missed seminars prove she lacks motivation.” Complete the repair:

“That sentence turns an observation into an explanation. Before choosing an explanation, I would add the ______ lens by asking ______, and I would add the ______ lens by asking ______.”

Use two different lenses. Then state one fact that could make you revise your preferred explanation.
:::

**Feedback.** Many pairs can work. A person-lens question might ask what Noor wants from the course and how she understands the change. A system-lens question might ask how night work and attendance rules constrain her options. A brain-lens question might ask what health or sleep evidence is missing. Your answer is strong if each blank contains a genuine question rather than a disguised conclusion, and if the revision fact could actually alter your account.

## Supported boundary problem

Mina is fictional. A student group wants to build a classroom presentation about how noise, discrimination, social support, and university procedures might affect her participation.

1. Choose three lenses and write one question from each.
2. Apply the scope test. Who is affected, what is the action, what are the possible consequences, and what governed route—if any—is required?
3. Now change one detail: the group plans to interview a real classmate about private health experiences and publish the recording. Which new boundary questions arise?

**Feedback.** The fictional presentation can remain academic if it analyzes questions and evidence without diagnosing Mina. The changed task introduces a real person, privacy, consent, publication rights, and possible harm. “It is for education” does not answer those concerns. Revise the activity to use a fully fictional case or a purpose-commissioned contribution governed by explicit consent and rights; do not improvise with a classmate's private story.

## Independent problem

Create a new fictional situation in two sentences, without borrowing from a real person's private experience. Then:

- write one question for each of the six lenses;
- identify the two lenses you would investigate first and explain why, without declaring the others irrelevant;
- propose one academic task and one action that would require a separate clinical route;
- justify both classifications with the four-question scope test.

Use this quality check: each lens should change the question being asked; each boundary decision should name the target and action; and at least one possible finding should be able to change your priorities.
