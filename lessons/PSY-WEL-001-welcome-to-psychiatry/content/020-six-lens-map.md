# Six lenses, six kinds of question

A **lens** is a disciplined way to ask a question. It directs attention, but it does not decide the answer in advance. The six lenses in this course help you keep distinct kinds of evidence visible.

| Lens | Question it contributes | What it must not become |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Mind** | What is the person experiencing, remembering, expecting, feeling, or trying to do? | A claim that an experience is “only in the mind.” |
| **Brain** | Which bodily processes, development, sleep, substances, illness, or treatment effects should be investigated? | A claim that a biological description is automatically the complete explanation. |
| **Person** | What are this individual's goals, strengths, history, identity, choices, and meanings? | A generic case with the person's priorities removed. |
| **Relationship** | How might family, friends, groups, care relationships, support, or conflict affect what happens? | An assumption that every difficulty began inside one person. |
| **Culture** | How do language, shared beliefs, identity, discrimination, and social meanings shape experience and response? | A stereotype applied to someone because of group membership. |
| **System** | How do housing, education, work, law, services, money, and policy alter risks and possibilities? | Background scenery that is noticed but never allowed to change the account. |

The table is a representation of *questions*, not a list of six causes. A lens may reveal a strong candidate explanation, a weak one, or no relevant contribution. Several lenses can also describe different parts of one chain. For Noor, you might ask whether night work changes opportunities for sleep, whether the new room affects rest, how she interprets the change, and what her course permits when attendance falls. Those questions do not establish what is happening. They identify evidence worth seeking.

## Integration without a winner-takes-all contest

Psychiatry is a medical specialty. One United States certification framework places bodily processes, psychological experience, and social context within the specialty's work. A global training statement reaches further into culture, public mental health, research, lived-experience participation, service systems, and supervised development. Together, these sources show why a serious curriculum cannot define the field as brain science alone or as conversation alone.

Integration does **not** mean that every explanation is compatible. Evidence may weaken or rule out a proposal. Nor does integration mean that every lens deserves equal space in every case. It means that the choice of lens is reasoned and revisable rather than automatic.

This course makes a further distinction:

- **Academic learning** builds knowledge and reasoning through sources, fictional cases, analyses, and simulations.
- **Clinical authority** concerns accountable actions with real people, such as assessment and treatment, within supervised and jurisdiction-specific professional arrangements.

Training and authorization differ by country and regulator. The six-lens map can travel across settings; a certification rule from one setting cannot.

:::{check}
:id: check-lens-versus-cause

Noor says the room is noisy, works nights, and reports poor sleep. Which response uses the six-lens map correctly?

A. “The system lens proves that housing caused everything.”

B. “All six lenses must contribute exactly one-sixth of the explanation.”

C. “Housing and work conditions generate system-level questions; mind, brain, person, relationship, and culture remain available if evidence makes them relevant.”

Choose before reading the feedback.
:::

**Feedback.** C treats the lenses as questions and leaves the weight of each explanation to evidence. A turns one useful question into a complete causal conclusion. B mistakes breadth for equal causal shares. If you chose A or B, rewrite the response using “ask whether” rather than “proves.”
