# Transfer the map to a policy question

A university reports that students returning after mental-health leave are less likely to complete the next term. An administrator proposes mandatory weekly symptom reports from every returning student.

The object of study is now a policy, not a fictional individual's difficulties. The six-lens map still works, but you must adapt the questions.

:::{check}
:id: check-policy-transfer

Before reading the guidance, write a short policy-analysis plan that includes:

1. at least four of the six lenses;
2. the evidence needed before claiming the policy would help;
3. the perspectives or goals of affected students;
4. one possible benefit and one possible harm;
5. a boundary sentence stating what your analysis can and cannot decide.
:::

## Guidance after your attempt

A mind-lens question could ask how the reporting requirement changes attention, fear, or willingness to seek support. A person-lens question could ask what returning students value and what routes they want. Relationship and culture questions could examine trust, stigma, language, and whose account is treated as credible. A system-lens question could examine privacy, accessibility, finances, service capacity, and the rule's effect on participation. A brain-lens question might be relevant only if a specific biological claim is actually proposed; the map does not require you to invent one.

Your evidence plan should distinguish the initial observation—lower completion—from a causal explanation. It should also include outcomes that could reveal harm, not only the outcome the administrator hopes to improve.

The work remains academic when you compare evidence, assumptions, rights, and possible system effects. It would cross into a different governed route if you used the policy exercise to assess a named student's mental state, direct that student's treatment, or claim professional authority over an individual decision.

## What successful transfer looks like

You have transferred the model if you can:

- select rather than mechanically list lenses;
- show how a policy changes the questions each selected lens asks;
- keep affected people's goals and system power visible;
- identify evidence that would change your conclusion;
- state the boundary between policy analysis and individual clinical judgment.

If your plan simply says “consider all factors,” revise it. Name the factor, the lens, the question, the evidence, and the limit.
