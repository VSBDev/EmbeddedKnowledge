# One result says yes

A research team studies a fictional marsh grass that survives salty soil. In a molecular assay, a protein called Tide-1 attaches to DNA beside a salt-response gene. The result is clean and repeatable. Does Tide-1 cause the whole plant to tolerate salt?

Not yet. The assay has revealed a possible step in a mechanism, but the headline claim crosses several gaps. Does that attachment change the gene? Does the change matter in a whole plant? Does it matter across natural populations? What happens when Tide-1 itself is deliberately changed?

This lesson is about crossing those gaps without pretending they have disappeared. You will combine molecular, whole-organism, population, and intervention evidence into one claim whose strength and limits match the evidence.

The central move is simple to say and demanding to do:

> Do not count studies. Ask what each line can show, what could distort it, whether the lines are genuinely independent, and exactly how far their agreement reaches.

The goal is not a universal evidence hierarchy. It is a defensible answer to a bounded question.

## Check the two prerequisites

Answer before reading the routing feedback.

1. A survey finds that plants carrying Tide-1 variant A are more likely to survive in salty estuaries. Name one reason the association might not be causal.
2. A cell model omits the plant's roots, vascular system, and soil microbes. What must an investigator say before using that model to explain survival of a whole plant?

If you answered that another variable could influence both variant frequency and survival, you activated the correlation–causation prerequisite. If you answered that the model's assumptions, scale, and missing processes limit the inference, you activated the models-and-limits prerequisite. Your wording need not match exactly.

## Recovery route

If either prerequisite is unavailable, take this ten-minute bridge before continuing.

- For association and causation, draw `Tide-1 variant → survival`. Add a third factor—such as estuary ancestry or local salinity—that could affect both. Until the design handles that rival path, use **associated with**, not **causes**.
- For model limits, complete four prompts: “This model represents ___; it leaves out ___; it is useful for predicting ___; it would fail if ___.”
- Then retry the two questions above. Continue when you can name one rival explanation for an association and one limit on carrying a model across scales.

You will not be asked to relearn either prerequisite in full. You will use both to decide how much weight each new evidence line deserves.
