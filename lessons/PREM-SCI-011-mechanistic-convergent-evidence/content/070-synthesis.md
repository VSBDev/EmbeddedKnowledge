# Rebuild the claim without the lesson

Close the earlier scenes. On a blank page, reconstruct the path from a research question to a calibrated claim. Use no more than seven lines.

Then answer two cumulative questions:

1. Where does the association–causation distinction enter the path?
2. Where do model assumptions and limits enter the path?

Do this before reading the reconstruction below.

## A compact reconstruction

1. State one bounded causal question with exposure, outcome, system or population, and timing.
2. Translate every study into the narrow result it actually supports.
3. Weigh reliability: design, measurement, missingness, and bias control.
4. Weigh relevance: match exposure, outcome, system, dose, time, and target population.
5. Compare consistency and map shared samples, tools, investigators, and assumptions.
6. Explain conflict or mark it as unresolved; identify the weakest causal or mechanistic bridge.
7. Write direction, strength, scope, pivotal support, uncertainty, exclusion, and next discriminating test.

Association versus causation enters when you translate each line and inspect its design. Model limits enter when you judge relevance across molecular systems, organisms, populations, and interventions. They return in the final scope statement.

If your reconstruction said only “collect four types and see whether they agree,” add weighting and dependence. If it listed every possible bias but never reached a claim, add the final integration step: uncertainty should calibrate a conclusion, not prevent all conclusions.

## Mastery check

The assessment contains two equivalent, untimed forms. Complete Form A first:

- `item-a-evidence-contribution`
- `item-a-convergence`
- `item-a-calibrated-claim`
- `item-a-conflict`

Each form is worth 16 points. Mastery-quality evidence requires at least 13 points and at least one point on every rubric criterion; a high total cannot compensate for omitting a whole dimension such as scope or conflict diagnosis. Attempt each item before opening its answer, reasoning, or common-error feedback.

If you do not yet meet the criterion, classify the error, use the route below, and complete Form B rather than replaying the same answers:

- `item-b-evidence-contribution`
- `item-b-convergence`
- `item-b-calibrated-claim`
- `item-b-conflict`

After both forms, a further retry should use a new fictional bundle with the same four demands: distinguish contributions, weigh convergence, calibrate a claim, and resolve a conflict. Changing only names or option order is not an adequate retry.

## Recovery route

- **You confuse evidence labels with rank.** For each line, write “can see” and “cannot see.” Remember that an intervention can also be organismal or population-level.
- **You treat association as causation.** Draw one plausible third variable that affects both exposure and outcome. Then ask what design feature blocks that path.
- **You treat a model as the target system.** Complete: “The model represents ___ under ___; it omits ___; carrying the result to ___ requires ___.”
- **You count studies.** Draw links between studies that share data, reagents, personnel, instruments, or assumptions. Reassess the remaining independent convergence.
- **You let randomization prove too much.** Name the assigned intervention, then audit concealment, blinding, adherence, attrition, measurement, target specificity, and scope.
- **You avoid a conclusion because one line conflicts.** Check question, timing, exposure delivery, and expected bias direction. Narrow the claim or name the unresolved hinge and a discriminating test.
- **Your claim sounds certain but says little.** Use the seven-part reconstruction and require a strength word with reasons, not by itself.

Return to Form B only after you can explain the repaired step aloud in ordinary language.

## Accessibility and alternatives

Every learning operation in this lesson has a text route. The diagram is reproduced as an ordered relationship in prose and as a table. No distinction depends on colour, position, animation, sound, hover, dragging, speed, or pointer use. Prompts, evidence bundles, feedback, citations, and assessment logic remain in semantic source order for keyboard, screen-reader, narrow-screen, 200% zoom/reflow, print, and reduced-motion use.

You may answer in prose, headings, bullets, or a table, and you may type or dictate using your own assistive technology. A diagrammed dependence map can be replaced by a list of study pairs and their shared feature. No physical field or laboratory action, image upload, audio, personal record, or personal health information is required. The supplied fictional datasets preserve the same evidence-integration operation as any visual or practical route.

## Schedule retrieval beyond today

- After about 48 hours, reconstruct the seven steps and write one sentence explaining why agreement across different biases matters.
- After about one week, take a scientific claim from your current reading and identify its strongest evidence line, weakest bridge, and unjustified word, if any.
- When you next study how claims depend on evidence and assumptions, retrieve the calibrated-claim structure before reading the new material. Use it to test whether an unstated assumption changes the claim's scope.

These delayed checks are learning opportunities, not proof of mastery by completion. The assessment and later transfer performance provide the evidence.
