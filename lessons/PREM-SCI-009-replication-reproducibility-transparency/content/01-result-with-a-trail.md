# When a result leaves a trail

A fictional plant-science lab reports that a mineral supplement made bean seedlings taller. The headline sounds simple. The path to it is not.

The researchers chose when to stop collecting observations. They decided which seedlings counted, which measurement summarized growth, and which analysis to show. Another team could try the same recipe, but only if the recipe is complete. Even then, one repeat would not turn the claim into a fact beyond doubt.

The useful question is not just **“Was the result positive?”** It is **“What trail lets another person test how much confidence the result deserves?”**

This lesson builds that trail from four safeguards:

1. **Preregistration** records the important question and analysis decisions before the outcomes are known.
2. **Open methods** make the procedure and analytic workflow inspectable, with appropriate protection for people and sensitive data.
3. **Replication** tests the claim with new data.
4. **Sensitivity analysis** asks whether the conclusion changes under other plausible assumptions or analytic choices.

They do different jobs. None is a certificate that a claim is true. Together they make hidden flexibility, missing detail, narrow conditions, and fragile conclusions easier to detect.

## Activate what you know about bias

Before reading further, answer from memory:

> A researcher tries several defensible outcome definitions after seeing the data, then reports only the definition that gives the largest difference. What made the result vulnerable to bias: the existence of several definitions, the timing of the choice, or the incomplete report?

All three matter, but in different ways. Several defensible definitions create **researcher degrees of freedom**. Choosing after seeing the result lets that flexibility favor a desired story. Reporting only the winning choice hides the dependence from the reader.

If that chain is hard to reconstruct, use this recovery route before continuing:

- Write the scientific question.
- List one choice that could change its answer.
- Mark whether the choice was made before or after outcomes were visible.
- Ask whether a reader can see the alternatives and their consequences.

That four-line audit is enough prerequisite knowledge for this lesson. You do not need to finish another resource first.

## Your destination

By the end, you will be able to inspect a study description, assign each safeguard its proper job, and design a transparent verification plan. You will also know where the safeguards stop: preregistration can preserve a poor plan, open files can expose an error without correcting it, a replication can disagree for several reasons, and a sensitivity analysis is only as informative as the alternatives it tests.
