# Build a verification plan in a new setting

A fictional bench-science team reports that light schedule L increases the day-10 biomass of one algal strain in its laboratory setup. You have only the claim, not the team's planning record, files, or follow-up evidence.

Before reading the model, design four checks. For each one, state what a person would do and what the result would allow them to conclude.

## One defensible transfer

**Preregister the decision trail.** Before day-10 biomass is known, register the bounded question, strain and growth conditions, primary outcome and time point, assignment and stopping rules, exclusions, missing-measurement plan, and primary comparison. Record later deviations rather than rewriting the plan.

**Open the method trail.** Share the light timing and intensity specification, culture and measurement protocol, material identifiers, variable dictionary, observations, analysis code, and software environment. If any information cannot be released, name what is withheld, why, and how appropriate access can be requested.

**Plan a new-data test.** Ask a second team to collect new cultures using the disclosed core method. State in advance which features should stay close and which change is intentional—such as a new operator or equipment unit—so the result tests a defined version of the claim.

**Plan sensitivity checks.** Identify plausible alternatives that still address day-10 biomass, such as justified handling of failed readings or another defensible model for the same comparison. Report how the conclusion varies across them instead of selecting the preferred estimate.

The four plans still do not answer whether L increases biomass. They specify evidence that would make the claim easier to audit, repeat, and calibrate.

## Mastery check

The assessment contains four untimed, retakeable items worth twelve points. Attempt each item before opening its answer and reasoning.

Mastery means at least **9 of 12 points** and at least one point on each item. That combination requires you to distinguish re-running from repeating, interpret sensitivity and replication together, protect participants while supporting inspection, and design all four safeguards in a new context.

If you miss an item, use its specific feedback:

- For same-data versus new-data confusion, circle the data source.
- For a sensitivity error, restate the unchanged scientific question and list only justified alternatives.
- For an openness error, separate inspectability from unrestricted public release.
- For a transfer gap, write the four audit questions first and fill one concrete action under each.

Then retry with the alternate scenario described in the answer record rather than memorizing a phrase.

## Synthesis

Carry this compact map into later reading:

> **Before:** Was the important path recorded before outcomes were known?  
> **Inspect:** Can another person understand and re-run what was done, within ethical access limits?  
> **New data:** Does an informative repeat raise or lower confidence in the bounded claim?  
> **Alternatives:** Does the conclusion depend on plausible assumptions or analytic choices?

The best conclusion is not the strongest sentence the evidence permits. It is the most accurately calibrated sentence: clear about what survived, what changed, and what remains uncertain.

## Accessibility and alternatives

Every relationship in the diagram is repeated in the four-row table and in the **before–inspect–new data–alternatives** text map. The numeric results in the worked example appear in a table and are interpreted in sentences. No task requires color, dragging, hovering, audio, image recognition, speed, laboratory access, or disclosure of personal information.

You may answer in prose, bullets, or a table. You may type, dictate with your own device, or use assistive technology. The ordered questions provide the same learning route if the concept map is unavailable. In print, read the scenes in their numbered order; prompts always appear before their feedback.

## Recovery route

If the four terms blur together, ignore the labels and sort evidence into three bins: **same data**, **new data**, or **decision timing**. Same-data regeneration is computational reproducibility; new-data testing is replication; a before-outcomes record is preregistration. Then ask whether alternative assumptions changed the same question—that is sensitivity analysis.

If “open” sounds like “public without limits,” write two columns: *needed for inspection* and *unsafe or unlawful to release*. Add de-identification, controlled access, or a data-use agreement where appropriate, and explain the route.

If disagreement feels like a verdict, write at least two possible explanations and one scope boundary before changing confidence. Do not infer misconduct from disagreement alone.

If sensitivity analysis feels like result shopping, declare the alternatives because they are plausible before comparing their results. Report the spread, including inconvenient outcomes.
