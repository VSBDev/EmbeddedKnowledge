# Authoring brief: PREM-LPP-003

This authoring record is not learner-facing instruction and does not count as an independent review.

## Target and learner

- **Target outcome:** `topic-orientation-mastery-and-retrieval` (`PREM-01.03`) — Design retrieval practice and spacing around durable performance rather than familiarity.
- **Declared learner:** a foundation-level prospective Premed learner who can already use a low-stakes diagnostic to classify knowledge as unfamiliar, fragile, or secure.
- **Required prerequisite:** `topic-orientation-diagnostic-baseline` (`PREM-01.02`).
- **Scope:** design a retrieval-and-spacing plan for bounded academic knowledge; interpret performance over attempts; adjust the plan after errors.
- **Exclusions:** no claim that one spacing interval is universally optimal; no individualized cognitive diagnosis; no clinical, credentialing, or admissions advice; no claim that self-study metrics alone prove competence.

## Prerequisite activation and recovery

The diagnostic asks the learner to classify one target and justify the classification from a recent unaided attempt. A learner who cannot do this is routed back to `topic-orientation-diagnostic-baseline` before continuing. The route is non-punitive: the baseline is information for plan design, not a grade.

## Central question and explanatory model

**Anchor question:** A learner can follow a page today but cannot reconstruct its reasoning two days later. What practice plan would generate evidence of durable performance?

The target model has five linked propositions:

1. Define mastery as an observable target performance under stated conditions, not a feeling of familiarity.
2. Elicit that performance from memory before reopening the source.
3. Compare the attempt with an answer model or rubric, diagnose the error, and take a specific recovery action.
4. Retrieve again after a delay and with some contextual variation so later performance, rather than immediate fluency, informs the mastery judgment.
5. Adapt support, delay, and mixture to the learner's performance and the required retention interval. The worked schedule is one defensible example, not a universal formula.

## Observable objectives and mastery evidence

- **Objective 1:** distinguish familiarity-producing activity from retrieval that yields evidence about later performance. Evidence: discriminate cases and explain the governing distinction.
- **Objective 2:** construct a retrieval plan that specifies target performance, initial attempt, feedback, recovery, delayed attempts, contextual variation, and a decision rule. Evidence: produce and justify a bounded plan from a diagnostic state and target date.
- **Objective 3:** interpret a sequence of attempts and adapt support, spacing, or task mixture without declaring mastery from one success. Evidence: analyze performance data and prescribe the next attempt.

Mastery evidence for this lesson is a defensible design plus reasoning across more than one task. Completion, rereading fluency, confidence, or one correct response is not accepted as sufficient evidence.

## Learner difficulties and discriminating cases

- **Evidence-backed high-value error:** treating ease during rereading as durable mastery. The repair contrasts immediate familiarity with later unaided reconstruction.
- **Boundary:** retrieval without corrective feedback can preserve an error; feedback without a prior attempt does not perform the retrieval operation.
- **Boundary:** interleaving is useful when selecting among already introduced categories or strategies; it is not a universal replacement for supported initial acquisition.
- **Boundary:** a calendar entry alone is not a retrieval task; the scheduled action must name what the learner will generate and how it will be judged.

## Representations and their jobs

- **Words:** define the design procedure and explain why each component belongs.
- **Planning table:** coordinate target, timing, prompt variation, feedback, and decision rule in one auditable plan.
- **Attempt record:** connect observed performance to the next planning decision.
- **Ordered checklist:** provide an equivalent linear route for narrow screens, print, and nonvisual reading.

No image, diagram, audio, video, personal data, or physical activity is needed.

## Worked example and practice progression

The complete example uses a fictional learner preparing to explain a scientific mechanism in fourteen days. It exposes the target performance, diagnostic evidence, selected retrieval tasks, an example sequence of days, feedback, checks, interpretation, and a self-explanation prompt. All topic details are invented at a generic level so the example does not teach unsupported biology.

This lesson uses the following project-authored practice progression to implement fading; the evidence supports worked guidance and performance-sensitive reduction of assistance, not this exact sequence as a fixed empirical ladder:

1. completion task: fill one missing plan component;
2. supported task: choose a retrieval action and justify it using a prompt;
3. independent task: design a short plan from a fragile baseline;
4. mixed selection: distinguish retrieval, feedback, restudy, and interleaving decisions;
5. transfer: redesign a peer study session under a new time and coordination constraint.

## Feedback, transfer, retention, and remediation

Feedback states correctness, names the principle, and gives a next action. Retakes change the target topic, initial diagnostic pattern, and time horizon so they require renewed reasoning.

The transfer task asks a study group to replace a rereading meeting with individual retrieval, comparison, targeted recovery, and a later changed-context check. The new group-coordination constraint requires adaptation rather than changed labels alone.

Delayed retrieval links:

- retrieve the `PREM-01.02` baseline classification while planning the first attempt;
- in later Premed lessons, revisit the same planning template after at least one delay and with domain-specific prompts;
- use later mixed assessments to require selection among confusable models only after each model has initial support.

### External evidence dossier

- **Retrieval:** Roediger and Karpicke (2006), DOI `10.1111/j.1467-9280.2006.01693.x`, is a primary prose experiment comparing repeated study and retrieval at immediate and delayed tests. Adesope, Trevisan, and Sundararajan (2017), DOI `10.3102/0034654316689306`, is a meta-analysis of practice testing. The studies and tests are heterogeneous, so neither record guarantees an effect for every task or learner.
- **Spacing:** Cepeda et al. (2006), DOI `10.1037/0033-2909.132.3.354`, quantitatively synthesizes distributed practice in verbal recall and the relation between spacing and desired retention interval. It does not supply one universal retrieval schedule, and its base concerns distributed presentations more broadly than retrieval alone.
- **Interleaving:** Brunmair and Richter (2019), DOI `10.1037/bul0000209`, analyzes interleaving and its moderators. Material similarity and task type constrain the result, so the lesson uses interleaving conditionally for discrimination after initial acquisition.
- **Feedback:** Wisniewski, Zierer, and Hattie (2020), DOI `10.3389/fpsyg.2019.03087`, meta-analyzes educational feedback and reports substantial heterogeneity. The lesson uses correctness, process, and next-action information as a bounded design model, not a universal feedback script.
- **Worked examples and fading:** Barbieri et al. (2023), DOI `10.1007/s10648-023-09745-1`, meta-analyzes worked examples in mathematics; Crissman (2006), durable identifier `AAI3208114`, meta-analyzes worked-example features including faded solution steps in structured domains; Tetzlaff et al. (2025), DOI `10.1016/j.learninstruc.2025.102142`, meta-analyzes assistance-by-prior-knowledge interactions. Together they support worked guidance and reducing assistance when learner performance can sustain more unaided work, not the lesson's exact five-stage progression.
