# Repair the fluency-equals-mastery model

First commit to a prediction: after two smooth rereadings, Lee can explain every displayed step while the page is open. Lee has not attempted the explanation without the page. Should Lee mark the target secure and move on? Write **yes** or **no**, then give one reason.

:::{misconception}
:id: misconception-fluency-equals-mastery
:label: “If it feels easy now, I have mastered it”

**The plausible model.** Ease while reading seems informative because effort has fallen and each sentence is recognizable. The model infers that lower effort during exposure means the knowledge will be available later.

**What the model cannot explain.** Repeated exposure can produce strong immediate fluency while a later closed-source attempt remains incomplete. The open page supplies cues and relations that the later task may withhold. Therefore the observation and the intended conclusion occur under different conditions.

**Replacement model.** Treat fluency as an experience during study, not as sufficient mastery evidence. Define the future operation, attempt it without the answer source, compare the generated response with criteria, repair the first material error, and collect another attempt after delay or meaningful variation.

**Compare on the same case.** Under the fluency model, Lee marks the target secure because reading is smooth. Under the performance model, Lee records “evidence missing,” closes the page, attempts the explanation, and lets the observed causal omissions determine recovery.

**Recheck in a new case.** Morgan watches a complete worked solution and can predict each visible next line. The final task will require selecting and executing the method without prompts in six days. What evidence is missing? A strong answer says that Morgan has not yet selected and executed the method unaided, either now or after delay. The next action is an unaided attempt followed by criterion-based feedback, not a confidence rating alone.
:::

If your opening prediction was **yes**, do not erase it. Write the observation that changed the inference: the open source supplies cues that the future closed-source performance withholds. Then schedule one attempt that tests the intended operation.

If your prediction was **no** but your reason was only “rereading is bad,” refine it. Rereading can be a recovery action when it targets a diagnosed gap. The error is using exposure fluency as sufficient evidence, not the mere presence of rereading in a plan.

:::{source-note}
:claims: claim-mastery-performance, claim-retrieval-durability, claim-feedback-recovery
:sources: source-authoring-evidence, source-content-standard, source-roediger-karpicke-2006, source-adesope-et-al-2017, source-wisniewski-et-al-2020

The repository synthesis reports that repeated study can look favorable on immediate performance while retrieval supports later retention under studied conditions. The repair therefore compares evidence conditions rather than banning rereading. Feedback and a renewed attempt remain necessary because retrieval without correction can leave an error intact.
:::
