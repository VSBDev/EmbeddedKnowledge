# From feeling familiar to performing later

You read an explanation twice. The second reading is smooth: every sentence seems recognizable, and the worked steps are easy to follow. Two days later, with the page closed, you cannot reconstruct the central relation. Which moment should guide your study plan—the smooth rereading or the later attempt?

This lesson builds a plan around the later operation. You will define what you must be able to do, attempt that performance from memory, use feedback to repair the attempt, and schedule changed retrieval opportunities across time. The result is not a magic calendar. It is an evidence loop in which observed performance changes the next study action.

By the end, you will be able to:

1. distinguish activity that produces familiarity from retrieval that tests a declared performance;
2. construct a retrieval-and-spacing plan from a diagnostic baseline and a retention horizon; and
3. adapt support, timing, and task mixture from a sequence of attempts without declaring mastery from one success.

In this lesson, **mastery** is a provisional, bounded judgment: the learner has repeatedly reconstructed or used the target knowledge without the source, after delay and with relevant variation. That operational definition is deliberately stricter than “I recognize this” and deliberately narrower than a claim of permanent knowledge or professional competence.

:::{callout}
:kind: boundary
:label: Scope and exclusions

The lesson teaches academic study-plan design. It does not supply a universally optimal spacing interval, diagnose memory ability, guarantee an examination result, award university credit, or establish clinical competence. The fictional study cases contain no medical or personal data.
:::

:::{source-note}
:claims: claim-mastery-performance, claim-retrieval-durability
:sources: source-authoring-evidence, source-content-standard

The repository evidence synthesis supports retrieval for later retention across varied educational conditions and warns that familiarity and immediate fluency are not durable-learning evidence. The project standard turns that evidence into the bounded performance model used here. Effects and useful task forms still vary by learner, material, feedback, and final assessment.
