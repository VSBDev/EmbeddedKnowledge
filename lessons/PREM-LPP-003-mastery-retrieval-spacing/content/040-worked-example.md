# Build a fourteen-day retrieval plan

The following case is fictional and teaches planning, not biology. Its day gaps illustrate one defensible schedule; they are not claimed to be optimal.

:::{worked-example}
:id: worked-example-fourteen-day-plan
:label: From fragile baseline to changed-case performance

**Task.** Ari has fourteen days before a seminar. The seminar requires an unaided explanation of a previously taught scientific mechanism and selection of that mechanism for a new bounded case. Ari's baseline is **fragile**: immediately after study, Ari can name the five parts but omits two causal links when explaining why one change leads to the next.

**Represent the givens, constraints, and unknowns.**

- Givens: fourteen-day horizon; five-part mechanism already introduced; two causal links missing; future task includes explanation and selection for a new case.
- Constraints: attempts must be unaided before feedback; the plan must fit ordinary individual study; no one spacing interval is assumed universally best.
- Unknowns: whether the missing links can be reconstructed after recovery, whether the mechanism can be selected for a changed case, and how much support later attempts require.

**Select the model.** The six-part retrieval loop fits because the goal is later generation and selection, not simply exposure. Ari needs a complete initial model of the planning procedure, then decreasing support and changed cases.

**Plan.** Define criteria, retrieve now, diagnose and repair the two missing links, then collect evidence across separated and varied attempts. Use each observation to choose the next task.

| Opportunity | Operation before review | Feedback and recovery | Decision rule |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Day 0 | Write the five-part mechanism as an ordered causal explanation; underline each “because” relation. | Compare against five part criteria and four link criteria. Study one worked explanation only for each link missing in this new attempt, then close it and reconstruct the chain once. | If any essential link remains absent, repeat a related supported retrieval before increasing the gap. |
| Day 2 | Reconstruct the mechanism as a labelled relation list rather than the original prose. | Check whether every causal link survives the representation change. Explain aloud or in writing why each link is needed. | If the chain is correct only in the original wording, vary representation again before adding model-selection practice. |
| Day 6 | Given two already introduced mechanisms and a new case, select one and justify the selection from its conditions. | Compare the justification with the selection criteria; target any confused boundary with a contrasting pair. | If selection conditions are confused, return briefly to blocked case comparison and schedule another changed case sooner. |
| Day 10 | Explain the mechanism to a fictional novice in six sentences, including one boundary where it would not apply. | Use the same causal and boundary criteria; replace vague labels with the specific relation they refer to. | If the explanation is accurate after delay and variation, remove remaining prompts and retain the final cumulative check. |
| Day 13 | Complete an unaided changed-case explanation and selection under the seminar's expected format. | Compare with the full rubric and record the first error, not just a score. | If accurate, carry one later cumulative retrieval into the course. If not, repair the exact relation and renew retrieval; do not hide the error by rereading repeatedly. |

**Execute the first decision.** Ari's fresh day-0 attempt includes all five parts and recovers one of the two links that were missing at baseline, but it still misses the relation between parts two and three. The new attempt therefore replaces the baseline error count as the evidence governing the next action. Ari does not label the whole mechanism “almost mastered”; Ari studies a worked account of the one link still missing, explains why the link is necessary, closes the source, and reconstructs the chain.

**Check the plan.** Every opportunity names an unaided operation. Feedback follows rather than precedes the attempt. Later tasks vary representation, selection, audience, or case. The final opportunity resembles the required performance without copying the original example. The plan leaves room to change after errors.

**Interpretation.** One successful reconstruction on day 0 would show that the immediate repair can be generated. It would not show that Ari can still explain and select the mechanism later. Evidence becomes more relevant to the seminar goal as performance survives delay, reduced support, and a changed case.

**Self-explanation prompt.** Which single design decision prevents Ari's day-0 success from being mistaken for mastery? State why.
:::

The key decision is to predeclare later changed retrieval opportunities and judge those performances. The exact numbered days are replaceable. What must remain is the alignment among target performance, observed errors, feedback, delayed variation, and the next-step rule.

For a non-tabular route, read each row as four sentences: on the named day, Ari retrieves before review; compares the response with criteria; repairs a specific gap; then lets that result determine the next opportunity. Nothing depends on the table's position or visual layout.
