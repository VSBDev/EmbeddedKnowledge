# The same difference can support different claims

Two research summaries land in your inbox.

- In one course, students who **chose** to use a flashcard app scored eight points higher than students who did not.
- In another course, eligible students were **randomly offered** the app or offered it after the final exam. The first group scored three points higher on average.

Both summaries report a difference in scores. They do not answer the same scientific question. The first tells you that app use and scores travelled together in that group. The second may tell you what changed because access was offered, provided the assignment and follow-up were carried out well.

That gap between “travelled together” and “changed because of” is the work of this lesson. You will learn to judge three kinds of conclusion:

1. **Association:** what varies with what?
2. **Mechanism:** what process could carry a change from a proposed cause to an outcome, and what evidence supports that process?
3. **Causal attribution:** compared with a relevant alternative, what would change if the exposure were changed?

The point is not to repeat “correlation is not causation” and stop. The useful skill is deciding what a particular design *does* support, what rival explanation remains, and how narrow the conclusion must be.

:::{check}
:id: check-prerequisite-design
:kind: diagnostic

Before reading on, answer from memory:

1. In each summary, who determined whether a student had app access: the student, the researcher, or an outside rule?
2. Which summary is observational, and which is experimental?
3. Name the exposure and outcome in both summaries.

Pause before checking the routing feedback below.
:::

If you identified self-selection in the first summary and researcher random assignment in the second, your study-design prerequisite is ready. In both summaries, the exposure is app access or use and the outcome is the exam score. If those distinctions were uncertain, use the recovery route before continuing; the rest of the lesson builds on them.

## Recovery route

Review the published lesson [Choosing a study design: assignment, selection, and time](https://embeddedknowledge.io/premed/lessons/read/?lesson=PREM-SCI-004). Focus on who creates the comparison, then return and classify the two summaries again. You may also use this three-line check:

- **Researcher assigns exposure:** experiment.
- **People or circumstances determine exposure:** observational study.
- **An outside rule or event determines exposure:** natural-experiment candidate whose assignment process still needs checking.

This route is non-punitive. Do not continue until you can identify exposure, outcome, and assignment without relying on the study's label.

## Scope for this lesson

You will judge causal language at an introductory level. You will not estimate formal causal models, perform multivariable regression, prove an instrumental variable valid, or decompose direct and indirect effects. Those tasks need additional assumptions and methods. This lesson is educational and does not establish clinical advice, professional authorization, or the safety or effectiveness of any intervention.
