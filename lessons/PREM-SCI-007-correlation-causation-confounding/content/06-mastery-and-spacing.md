# Make the strongest claim the design can carry

Use this compact audit whenever a study summary invites causal language:

1. **Name the target.** Is the claim about association, a mechanism, or a change under an intervention?
2. **Define the contrast.** What exposure or assignment is compared with what alternative, for which outcome and population?
3. **Inspect assignment and time.** Who or what set the exposure, and did the proposed cause precede the outcome?
4. **Map rival paths.** Which pre-exposure common causes, reverse arrows, selection steps, measurement differences, or missing outcomes remain?
5. **Place intermediates correctly.** Is a third variable a confounder to block, a mediator on the route of interest, or a collider to leave unconditioned for this question?
6. **State the boundary.** Name the assignment, studied population, setting, outcome, period, and assumptions that limit the conclusion.
7. **Integrate without laundering.** Mechanism studies, negative controls, natural experiments, and other approaches contribute their own evidence; none silently borrows another design's strengths.

An association can be scientifically useful without being causal. A mechanism can be well supported without establishing a population effect. A causal estimate can be credible within a randomized or defensibly assigned comparison while its mechanism or transport to another setting remains uncertain. Precision means saying exactly which of those achievements the evidence earns.

## Mastery check

Complete the five mastery items without reopening earlier scenes. Keep the feedback closed until after you have made a genuine attempt.

The gate carries 15 points. Mastery requires at least 12 points and full credit on the final causal-attribution item, because an unsupported causal claim is the critical error for this outcome. A response below the threshold or an incomplete final item is “not yet”: follow the item-specific feedback, use the route below, and retry with the alternate context named in the answer reasoning. Retakes are untimed and unlimited.

## Recovery route

- If you confuse study labels, revisit [Choosing a study design](https://embeddedknowledge.io/premed/lessons/read/?lesson=PREM-SCI-004) and identify assignment before returning.
- If you state a causal conclusion too early, use a four-column table: **observed contrast | assignment | rival path | strongest claim**. Rework the app and laboratory cases one row at a time.
- If third-variable roles blur together, redraw only the arrowheads. Say “common cause,” “on the route,” or “common effect” before using the technical label.
- If you treat randomization as a guarantee, write “comparable in expectation” and add one implementation or population boundary.
- If mechanism and effect blend together, write two sentences: “This evidence supports the process link…” and “This design supports/does not support the outcome contrast…”.

## Accessibility and alternatives

All essential relationships appear in text as well as in the declarative diagram. You may answer every prompt by typing, speaking to a scribe, or drawing labelled arrows; no task depends on color, pointer movement, dragging, sound, animation, or a time limit. For the diagram, use the equivalent list: confounder $X \leftarrow C \rightarrow Y$; mediator $X \rightarrow M \rightarrow Y$; collider $X \rightarrow S \leftarrow Y$. Tables preserve the same source order in narrow-screen, zoomed, screen-reader, and print views. If symbolic arrows are hard to interpret, replace them with the phrases “$C$ causes both,” “$M$ carries the route,” and “both cause $S$.”
