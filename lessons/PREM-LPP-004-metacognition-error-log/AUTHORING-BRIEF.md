## Target and learner

- **Target outcome:** `topic-orientation-metacognition-and-error-log` (`PREM-01.04`) — Classify errors, calibrate confidence, and choose the next learning action from evidence.
- **Declared learner:** a foundation-level prospective Premed learner who can already define a target performance, retrieve before feedback, and plan delayed retrieval.
- **Required prerequisite:** `topic-orientation-mastery-and-retrieval` (`PREM-01.03`).
- **Scope:** interpret bounded, low-stakes study records; classify the first unsupported operation; compare confidence with scored performance; select a matching repair and fresh probe.
- **Exclusions:** no diagnosis of ability, disability, health, personality, or motivation; no validated universal error taxonomy; no claim that keeping a log by itself improves learning; no clinical, admissions, credit, or professional decision.

## Prerequisite activation and recovery

The diagnostic asks the learner to reconstruct the prior lesson's target → retrieve → compare → repair → retrieve-after-delay loop before viewing a criterion. A learner who cannot name the performance target, an attempt before feedback, and a later fresh probe returns to `topic-orientation-mastery-and-retrieval`. A learner who has the loop but cannot distinguish a result from a confidence judgment continues with the explicit calibration model in this lesson.

## Central question and explanatory model

**Anchor question:** Two incorrect answers receive the same score. One learner chose the wrong model; another chose the right model but made an execution error. Why should their next study actions differ?

The target model has five linked propositions:

1. Metacognitive monitoring produces a judgment about current performance evidence; metacognitive control selects a next action from that judgment.
2. Confidence becomes calibration evidence only when it predicts performance on the same declared scoring rule and scale used to observe the result.
3. An error classification is a provisional hypothesis about the earliest unsupported operation in a chain: interpret, retrieve, select, execute, or verify.
4. A useful error-log entry links observation to hypothesis, targeted action, and a fresh probe. The entry is a decision aid, not evidence of mastery by itself.
5. New performance evidence can revise both the error hypothesis and the confidence judgment; neither becomes a stable label for the learner.

Every fictional record that derives an observed score from process evidence declares its weights and any dependency among criteria. In particular, model-execution credit is awarded only for accurate execution of a model applicable under the stated condition. This makes each derived percentage and signed gap independently reproducible.

## Observable objectives and mastery evidence

- **Objective 1:** classify a bounded error by locating the earliest unsupported operation and justify uncertainty or a discriminating probe.
- **Objective 2:** calculate and interpret a signed confidence–performance gap without treating one gap as a trait or mastery decision.
- **Objective 3:** select a next learning action that targets the diagnosed operation and specify a fresh probe that could confirm or revise the diagnosis.

Mastery evidence is a defensible classification–calibration–action chain across multiple records, including one transfer redesign. A completed table, a plausible label, confidence alone, or one corrected answer is insufficient.

## Learner difficulties and discriminating cases

- **High-value boundary:** the same wrong final answer can originate at interpretation, model selection, or execution; the output alone does not locate the first failed operation.
- **High-value boundary:** a predicted rubric percentage can be compared with the resulting rubric percentage, whereas a probability that the whole answer is correct cannot be subtracted meaningfully from a partial-credit score.
- **High-value boundary:** high predicted rubric performance with weak observed performance is a positive gap on that task, not proof of a fixed overconfident personality.
- **High-value boundary:** a low predicted rubric percentage with strong observed performance calls for calibration feedback and later verification, not unnecessary reteaching of content already demonstrated.
- **Near miss:** “reread the whole chapter” may expose the learner to relevant material but does not target a known selection or execution error and does not itself test whether repair occurred.
- **Uncertainty case:** when the record lacks a visible plan or intermediate steps, the correct classification is unresolved and the next action is a discriminating probe rather than a guessed category.

## Representations and their jobs

- **Operation chain:** an ordered text sequence—interpret → retrieve → select → execute → verify—locates the earliest broken link without implying a universal psychological taxonomy.
- **Calibration table:** places confidence, rubric performance, and signed gap on one scale and preserves task/context labels.
- **Error-log row:** coordinates observation, provisional category, next action, and fresh probe in an auditable decision record.
- **Linear checklist:** provides the same operation in source order for narrow screens, print, and nonvisual reading.

No image, audio, video, third-party dataset, personal record, or physical activity is needed.

## Worked example and practice progression

The worked example uses an invented two-model task whose rules and four equally weighted rubric criteria are supplied in the scene. Execution credit is explicitly contingent on selecting an applicable model, so the 50% observed score and positive 40-point gap follow from the declared rule. The example exposes the task, givens, unknown, selected monitoring/control model, plan, ordered analysis, calibration calculation, independent check, interpretation, and self-explanation prompt.

Practice fades as follows:

1. completion task: fill the missing observation-to-category link;
2. supported comparison: distinguish selection from execution using visible intermediate steps;
3. independent cases: classify, calibrate, and choose actions without a category prompt;
4. mixed selection: decide between targeted restudy, contrasting cases, step checking, and confidence feedback;
5. transfer: redesign a score-only weekly review into a privacy-preserving evidence-to-action system.

Feedback states whether the chain is supported, names the first unresolved operation, and prescribes the next discriminating probe. Retakes use new fictional task rules and new evidence patterns.

## Source dossier

### `source-debruin-monitoring-control`

- **Source:** de Bruin, Dunlosky, and Cavalcanti (2017), *Monitoring and regulation of learning in medical education: the need for predictive cues*.
- **Exact support:** the monitoring/control framework; the reciprocal relation between monitoring judgments and control decisions; the importance of cues that predict later performance rather than cues that merely feel fluent.
- **Scope and uncertainty:** a scholarly review focused on medical education. It does not validate this lesson's five error categories or guarantee that any log format improves performance.
- **Use and rights:** factual reference only; the explanation, examples, operation chain, and assessment are independently created. The PMC open-access record displays CC BY.
- **Agent access:** NCBI's current policy and usage guidance were checked before substantive access on 2026-07-20. No term prohibiting this limited agent access was found. No access control was bypassed.

### `source-carpenter-calibration-training`

- **Source:** Carpenter et al. (2019), *Domain-general enhancements of metacognitive ability through adaptive training*.
- **Exact support:** in the reported controlled study, feedback directed at repeated trial-level metacognitive judgments improved calibration assessed with a quadratic score equal to one minus the Brier score on the trained perceptual task relative to performance-only feedback and generalized to an untrained recognition-memory task; the study also analysed metacognitive efficiency with signal-detection indices.
- **Scope and uncertainty:** small experimental groups, laboratory perceptual and memory tasks, repeated trial-level judgments, and one training design. Its quadratic and signal-detection measures are not the lesson's single-task signed predicted-minus-observed rubric gap, so it does not validate that quantity, establish how it changes, show universal educational transfer, or support a stable personal trait.
- **Use and rights:** factual reference only; no stimulus, dataset, figure, table, or wording is reused. The PMC open-access record displays CC BY.
- **Agent access:** NCBI terms and usage guidance checked 2026-07-20; no agent restriction found for this limited access.

### `source-chen-retrieval-judgments`

- **Source:** Chen, Zhang, and Liu (2019), *Retrieval Practice Facilitates Judgments of Learning Through Multiple Mechanisms*.
- **Exact support:** in the reported experiments, judgments made after test trials corresponded more closely with later accuracy than judgments after restudy; retrieval confidence and fluency were partial mediators.
- **Scope and uncertainty:** laboratory memory materials and prospective judgments of learning. The result supports using observable retrieval as one monitoring cue, not the claim that retrieval is the only valid cue in every domain.
- **Use and rights:** factual reference only; no stimulus, dataset, figure, table, or wording is reused. The PMC open-access record displays CC BY.
- **Agent access:** NCBI terms and usage guidance checked 2026-07-20; no agent restriction found for this limited access.
