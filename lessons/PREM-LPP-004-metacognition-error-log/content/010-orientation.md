# Turn an error into a next decision

Two learners submit the same wrong answer. One interpreted the prompt accurately and remembered the needed rules but selected the wrong model. The other selected the right model and made an execution error. The score is the same, but the most useful next action is not.

This lesson turns a study result into a bounded decision. **Metacognitive monitoring** (`term-metacognitive-monitoring`) asks what the current evidence supports. **Metacognitive control** (`term-metacognitive-control`) uses that judgment to choose what to do next. You will connect them with a small error log that records evidence, a provisional error hypothesis, confidence calibration, a targeted action, and a fresh probe.

By the end, you will be able to:

1. locate the earliest unsupported operation in a response;
2. compare confidence with scored performance on the same task; and
3. choose a repair and a new probe that could confirm or revise the diagnosis.

The lesson does **not** teach a universal psychological taxonomy, diagnose a person, prove that logging causes learning, or establish permanent mastery. It does not support admissions, clinical, credit, or professional decisions. Its categories are a useful study-decision model whose adequacy must be tested against new performance.

## Route through the lesson

First reconstruct the durable-performance loop with the self-contained diagnostic in this lesson; the graph identifies `topic-orientation-mastery-and-retrieval` as the prerequisite outcome, but no separate lesson pack is assumed to be available. Then build the monitoring–control model, learn the calibration calculation, inspect a complete worked record, repair a score-only response, practise with fading support, and redesign a weekly review system. The final assessment uses new fictional evidence.

## Accessibility and alternatives

Every essential relation appears in source-order prose. Tables can be read one row at a time or rewritten with one field per line. No task depends on color, position, sound, motion, hover, dragging, handwriting, or speed. Responses may be typed, spoken to text, dictated to a scribe, or supplied in another structured text form while preserving the same reasoning. Use fictional or learner-owned records; do not enter names, grades linked to identity, health information, disability details, or another person's private data.
