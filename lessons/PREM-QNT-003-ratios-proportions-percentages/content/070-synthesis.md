# Reconstruct the reference-first method

1. Attach labels and preserve ratio order.
2. State why proportional scaling is justified.
3. Name the whole or positive starting value that supplies the denominator.
4. Check that a relative comparison has a meaningful zero; otherwise choose an absolute or domain-specific comparison.
5. Calculate with a fraction before converting to percent.
6. Reverse, substitute, or bound the answer.

:::{check}
:id: check-synthesis-reference-first

Answer before reading the feedback:

- Why are $3:5$ and $6:10$ equivalent?
- What assumption makes a proportion appropriate?
- In ordinary percent change, why is the old value the denominator?
- Why is “100% increase from $10\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}$ to $20\ ^\circ\mathrm{C}$” misleading?
- How do percentage points differ from relative percent change?

**Feedback after an attempt:** The first ratio is multiplied term-by-term by 2, so its quotient is unchanged. A proportion needs a justified constant multiplier or constant rate. Ordinary percent change compares the difference with a positive starting value. Celsius has an arbitrary zero, so ratios of Celsius readings change when the origin changes. Percentage points subtract two percentages; relative percent change divides their difference by the starting percentage.
:::

Then complete `assessment.json`. If ratio order fails, return to “Keep the ratio labels attached.” If the wrong denominator appears, redo the mixture transfer with every whole written explicitly. If arithmetic is correct but the comparison is unlabeled, add “percentage points” or “percent increase/decrease” before retrying. If the boundary item fails, return to the percent-change boundaries and ask whether the starting value is positive and its zero is meaningful.
