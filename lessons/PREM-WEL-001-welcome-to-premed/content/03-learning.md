# What learning will look like

You will not learn this science by reading definitions once. Study asks you to meet an explanation, retrieve it without looking, solve or model something, inspect evidence, receive feedback, and try again with less support.

Practical work may be a **physical investigation**, a **simulation**, or work with a supplied **dataset or observation**. All three can build question-design and analysis skills, but only physical work can show that you performed a hands-on action. Your course record will name which kind you completed, and this welcome requires no physical work.

Here is a preview using a fictional training dataset. It requires no prior science.

:::{worked-example}
:id: worked-example-flow-preview
:label: A model-flow question from start to finish

**Task.** A simulation pumps the same liquid for ten seconds through two equal-length tubes. Tube A is narrow and Tube B is wider.

| Run | Tube A | Tube B |
|---|---:|---:|
| 1 | 20 mL | 79 mL |
| 2 | 19 mL | 82 mL |
| 3 | 21 mL | 78 mL |

The question is: *Does tube width affect the volume collected in this model?*

**Represent what is given.** Width is the planned difference. Liquid, tube length, pump setting, and collection time are held constant. The result is volume after ten seconds.

**Choose a principle and plan.** A fair comparison changes the tested feature while keeping the other conditions the same. Compare all three runs, then keep the conclusion inside the simulation.

**Work through the evidence.** Tube A stays near 20 millilitres and Tube B near 80. Every Tube B result is about four times its paired Tube A result. The difference between tubes is much larger than the small run-to-run variation.

**Check the reasoning.** Units and collection times match, and no run reverses the pattern. We should still confirm that the simulation held the pump and liquid constant; otherwise width would not be the only planned explanation.

**Interpret and limit.** The wider tube allowed more liquid to pass in this model under these settings. The data do not establish an exact law or tell us what happens in a human blood vessel. Later physics, chemistry, and physiology add pressure, resistance, fluids, tissues, and biological regulation.

**Self-explanation.** Which choice protects the conclusion most: collecting a large number, using millilitres, or holding the other conditions steady? Why?
:::

The strongest answer is **holding the other conditions steady**, because that makes width the planned difference. Units make the measurement interpretable and repetition reveals variation, but neither fixes a comparison in which the pump or liquid also changed.

:::{check}
:id: check-faded-comparison
:kind: practice

A second dataset compares a narrow tube at a low pump setting with a wide tube at a high setting. Can it isolate the effect of width? Answer yes or no, name the competing difference, and propose a repair.
:::

After answering, compare: **No.** Pump setting changes with width, so either could explain the result. Repeat the comparison at one pump setting. If you named only the problem, add the repaired comparison.

This is the course's reasoning pattern in miniature: define the question, choose relevant ideas, work carefully, check, interpret, and state limits.
