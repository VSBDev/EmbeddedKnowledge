# How the subjects prepare you for what follows

The recommended journey has a broad order, although some subjects run together. Each stage supplies ideas for later explanations.

1. **Begin with scientific inquiry, laboratory practice, mathematics, and statistics.** You learn to frame a question, compare fairly, measure with units, read graphs, describe variation, and preserve an honest record. The same habits let you test a chemical prediction, interpret a genetics result, or challenge a population claim.
2. **Study general chemistry and introductory biology side by side.** Chemistry explains particles, bonds, reactions, solutions, and energy. Biology introduces molecules, membranes, cells, transport, and organization. Together they connect a biological outcome with interactions that could produce it.
3. **Add physics while chemistry and biology deepen.** Forces, energy, pressure, flow, electricity, waves, and light make measurements and body processes easier to model. Equilibrium, acids and bases, inheritance, and gene expression add regulation and continuity. These subjects help you test whether an explanation is chemically, physically, and biologically possible.
4. **Use organic chemistry to enter biochemistry.** Carbon-based structures, functional groups, three-dimensional shape, and reaction patterns prepare you to reason about proteins, membranes, nucleic acids, and metabolism. Biochemistry then shows how those molecules cooperate in a regulated cell and how evidence can test the explanation.
5. **Carry those foundations into human structure, physiology, microbiology, and immunology.** Tissues and organs depend on transport, energy, signalling, and control. Infection and defence depend on cells, molecules, evolution, and population change. Earlier ideas now help explain whole-body function.
6. **Broaden the account with behaviour, society, and population health.** People live in social and physical environments, so later medical study needs evidence about behaviour, institutions, access, inequality, and populations. This helps you distinguish a molecular mechanism, an individual experience, and a population pattern.
7. **Finish with integration and capstone work.** A new problem will not announce which subject to use. You must choose relevant ideas, connect them through cause or evidence, inspect limitations, and communicate a conclusion another person can audit.
