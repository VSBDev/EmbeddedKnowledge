# Make the connections yourself

The first example supplied the map. Now you will do more of the work yourself: recall the route, repair an incomplete map, and choose a route through a new question.

## Retrieve before you look back

:::{check}
:id: check-five-questions

Without reopening the first scene, write the five guiding questions in your own words. Then name the thread that should run through every subject.
:::

Make a real attempt before reading on.

Your answer may use different words. It should begin by asking how we know, then move through physical and chemical change, living cells and genetic information, and coordinated systems, people, and populations. It should end by asking whether the combined explanation works in a new problem. Scientific inquiry keeps returning: check measurements, reason from evidence, and notice the limits.

If you remembered only two or three parts, add the missing questions now. That repair is more useful than rereading the list without trying to recall it.

## A subject list is not yet an explanation

:::{misconception}
:id: misconception-subject-inventory

Consider this answer: “The course is mathematics, chemistry, biology, and physiology.”

Before reading further, say what the list leaves out.

The list tells us which subjects appear, but not what each subject helps explain or how one prepares you to use another. The same four labels could sit beside many unrelated questions.

Repair the answer by adding actions and connections: “Mathematics helps us judge measurements; chemistry helps us reason about molecular change; biology connects those changes to cells; physiology connects cells and organs in a whole-body response.” Now the reader can follow the work from one level to the next.

Recheck the idea with “chemistry and biochemistry.” Write one sentence that connects them instead of merely placing them side by side.
:::

A useful reply might say that chemistry supplies ideas about molecular structure, interaction, and change that biochemistry uses to explain processes in living systems. Your sentence can differ if it makes a clear, accurate connection.

## Practise with less support

**Completion problem.** Someone asks whether a change in genetic information could be connected to a change in movement. Complete these three lines without trying to answer the science:

1. Start with evidence by asking …
2. Connect two subject areas by asking how …
3. Check the proposed connection by looking for …

Your route could connect genetics to molecular and cell biology, then connect cells to muscles, nerves, or movement. Physics may also help if the question turns to force or motion. The point is not to include every subject. The point is to choose the subjects that change what you can ask or explain.

**Try a new kind of question.** Imagine a headline that says, “People who use a health app sleep better, so the app improves sleep.” Map a course route for investigating the claim. Name the first question you would ask, two subject areas that could contribute, the connection between them, and one result that would make you doubt the claim.

After you answer, check your route:

- Did you begin by asking what was measured and whether the headline claims more than the evidence supports?
- Did each chosen subject add a question or method, rather than serving as a label?
- Did you connect behaviour or biology to evidence and quantitative reasoning?
- Did you name a result that could make you revise the explanation?

If you wrote only a list, add a verb and a connection to every subject. If you included almost every subject, remove any one that does not change the investigation. Then try again with a different non-personal question.
