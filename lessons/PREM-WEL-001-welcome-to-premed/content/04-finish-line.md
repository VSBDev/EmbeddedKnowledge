# Use the map, then picture the finish line

The destination is not a shelf full of separate facts. It is the ability to use scientific ideas together and to show how you reached a conclusion.

By course completion, four verbs should describe real work you can do:

- **Explain.** Trace causes and consequences step by step, connect each step, and distinguish what the evidence supports from what it does not.
- **Model.** Make a deliberately simpler version of a situation, use it to predict or interpret something, and state what the model leaves out.
- **Investigate.** Turn a question into measurements or observations, choose a fair comparison, keep a usable record, and judge the limits of the result.
- **Integrate.** Choose ideas from more than one subject and connect molecules, cells, systems, people, or populations when the question requires it.

These abilities make the earlier subject journey useful. Inquiry keeps the evidence honest. Mathematics makes patterns and uncertainty clearer. Chemistry and physics provide models of matter, energy, forces, and movement. Biology and biochemistry connect those models to living systems. Human sciences and the final integration work ask how the pieces act together.

Completing the course does not qualify you to diagnose or treat anyone. It is preparation for later study, not a degree, academic credit, a medical qualification, or a guarantee of admission.

## Show that you have the map

Answer these three prompts without looking back. They ask you to orient yourself, not to recall scientific facts.

1. **Describe the journey.** In a short paragraph, describe at least four stages of the course and explain how one stage prepares you for another.
2. **Connect the subjects.** A report claims that a change in a protein causes a change in movement. Without deciding whether the claim is true, map at least three course lenses that could investigate it. Explain the connection between each lens and name evidence that could make you revise the route.
3. **Picture the finish line.** Describe what it would look like to explain, model, investigate, and integrate a scientific question. Then name two things that completing this course would not promise.

After you submit, read the feedback for each answer and find the missing part of your map. On a retry, use a different connection or a different fictional question instead of memorising the sample answer.

## Keep the map alive

Return to the five guiding questions when you begin chemistry, biology, physiology, and the final integration work. Ask: What can this subject explain now? Which earlier idea does it use? What evidence would connect the two? Rebuilding one connection after a delay will tell you more than simply recognizing the course list.

## Recovery route

If the subject journey still feels like a long list, reduce it to three moves:

1. Check the evidence.
2. Connect small-scale processes to cells and systems.
3. Test the combined explanation in a new problem.

Return to the worked running example and label those three moves. Then choose one new question and repeat them. Once that shorter route is clear, add the five detailed questions from the opening scene.

If a subject name is unfamiliar, you do not need to study that subject before finishing this welcome. Use the ordinary-language question beside it and continue.

## Accessibility and alternatives

This lesson does not require exercise, personal health measurements, or private information. Every scenario is fictional. You may replace any scenario with a non-personal question that is easier or safer for you to use.

You can answer in sentences, bullets, a spoken response, or a text-only outline. Keep the connections in a clear order and name them in words; no meaning depends on colour, a diagram, dragging, sound, or a timed response.
