# Welcome to Premed

Welcome to Premed. You are beginning a journey through science that makes the human body more understandable and evidence more trustworthy.

You need not know these subjects yet. This welcome shows what you will learn, why each earns its place, and what you can do when they connect. The course begins with careful questions, moves through matter and living systems, and ends with you using evidence on unfamiliar problems.

Here is one glimpse of a finished explanation. If a wider tube carries more liquid in a model, evidence tests the pattern, physics explains how width affects flow, and physiology asks how living tissues and regulation change the picture. Connecting those contributions and limits turns separate facts into an explanation.

Here is the journey ahead.

- **Scientific method and laboratory practice:** You will ask testable questions, choose variables and fair comparisons, measure carefully, keep reproducible records, and decide what a result supports. These habits keep evidence open to inspection and challenge.
- **Mathematics and statistics:** You will use units, ratios, graphs, probability, averages, variation, and uncertainty ranges to read research, compare groups, check answers, and separate a numerical pattern from a justified conclusion.
- **Physics:** You will use forces and motion; pressure, fluids, and transport; electricity; waves, sound, and light; heat; and radiation to explain movement, blood flow, electrical signals, hearing, vision, measurement, and imaging.
- **General chemistry:** You will connect atoms, bonds, molecular shape, reactions, amounts, gases, solutions, energy changes, reaction rates, equilibrium, acids, and bases to the molecular setting of cells and to laboratory, biology, and biochemistry calculations.
- **Organic chemistry:** You will trace how molecular shape, recurring groups, electron movement, and three-dimensional arrangement affect carbon-based molecules, and how separation and instrument patterns reveal structure. This prepares reasoning about why molecules of life bind and change.
- **Biochemistry:** You will connect proteins and enzymes, membranes, carbohydrates, fats, nucleotides, metabolism, and biological information to explain how cells use energy, regulate reactions, communicate, and respond to molecular change.
- **Biology and genetics:** You will follow cell organization, membranes, division, signalling, inheritance, gene expression, variation, and evolution to explain how living systems preserve information, develop different functions, and vary across individuals and populations.
- **Human structure and physiology:** You will connect tissues and organs to transport, internal control, nerves, hormones, movement, circulation, breathing, fluid balance, digestion, reproduction, and development. The aim is to explain whole-body mechanisms, not make clinical decisions.
- **Microbiology and immunology:** You will compare microbes and viruses, growth, transmission, evolution, barriers, rapid general defences, and slower targeted responses to explain infection and immunity across an agent, host, environment, and time.
- **Behaviour, society, and population health:** You will examine perception, learning, stress, relationships, institutions, inequality, access, environment, and population evidence. These help explain why biology alone cannot account for every pattern and why a group association is not one person's experience.
- **Integration and capstone work:** You will bring subjects together around an unfamiliar, non-diagnostic problem, inspect sources and data, calculate or model, explain mechanisms, communicate uncertainty, and revise after critique.

The destination is demanding but concrete. By completion, you should be able to **explain** important scientific relationships, **model** them with words, quantities, diagrams, or data, **investigate** questions with honest methods and records, and **integrate** several subjects when one alone is not enough.

Nobody expects you to begin at the end. Curiosity, careful practice, and a willingness to correct your reasoning are enough to start.
