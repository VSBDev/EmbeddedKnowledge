# Map one question before answering it

You do not need to know exercise physiology to see how the subjects connect. Here is a complete example of choosing the right questions before you know the science well enough to answer them.

:::{worked-example}
:id: worked-use-running-question

**Task.** A fictional learner records a quicker pulse and quicker breathing after a short run. Build a plan for explaining why the two observations belong in the same investigation. The task is to choose and connect the kinds of knowledge the question needs, not to diagnose anyone or explain the physiology in advance.

**What is given?** Two observations made before and after an activity.

**What is unknown?** The chain of causes connecting the activity to both changes, and whether the measurements are good enough to support an explanation.

**Choose a useful course map.** Begin with evidence because an explanation is only as good as the observations it must explain. Next ask about physical and molecular processes. Then build from cells to organs and the whole-body response. This map fits because the question crosses several levels.

**Plan.** Ask one useful question at each level, then state how the answers would connect.

1. **Begin with inquiry and measurement.** Ask how pulse and breathing were measured, what was recorded before the run for comparison, and whether repeated measurements show the same pattern.
2. **Add physics and chemistry.** Ask what is moving—such as air, blood, or electric charge—what makes it move, and how energy is carried, released, or used.
3. **Add biology and biochemistry.** Ask what active cells need during activity and which cell processes meet those needs.
4. **Add physiology.** Ask how breathing, circulation, nerves, and muscles could coordinate rather than treating each observation as a separate event.

**Check the plan.** Each subject should add a useful part to the same explanation. Their roles may overlap; the important thing is to state how each part connects to the next. A useful plan also names one possible result that would make you doubt or revise the explanation.

**Interpretation.** This map is not yet an explanation of exercise physiology. It shows why the course begins with evidence and foundations, then builds toward cells, systems, and integration. When the science has been learned, the same map can guide an explanation that is supported by evidence and honest about its limits.
:::

The decisive choice was not a formula or a fact. It was deciding to treat the observations as parts of one question and then choosing a sensible order in which to connect them.

Before moving on, answer in one sentence: **Which connection in the plan matters most, and what would be missing if you skipped it?** More than one answer can be sound. A strong answer names both sides of the connection and the gap that would remain.
