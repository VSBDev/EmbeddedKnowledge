# Why one question needs several sciences

Imagine reaching the top of three flights of stairs. Your pulse is loud, your breathing is quick, and one small question appears: what made all of that change together?

A good answer cannot stay inside one subject. You would need trustworthy observations. You would need to reason about movement, matter, and energy. Then you would need to connect molecules to cells, cells to organs, and organs to a whole person. This pattern of reasoning—from a question to a connected explanation—is the shape of this Premed course.

The course follows five linked questions:

1. **How do we know?** Scientific inquiry, mathematics, statistics, and laboratory practice help you decide what to measure, how much confidence to place in a result, and which conclusions the evidence can support.
2. **What is moving or changing?** Physics and chemistry help you reason about forces, fluids, electricity, waves, matter, and energy.
3. **What is a living system doing?** Biology and genetics help explain how cells use genetic information, reproduce, vary, and change. Organic chemistry and biochemistry connect molecular structure and reactions to what cells do.
4. **How do the parts work together?** Physiology, immunity, microbiology, behaviour, and society widen the view from molecules and cells to a person and a population.
5. **Does the explanation still work in a new problem?** Integration work and the final capstone—a task that brings earlier work together—ask you to choose the ideas that fit, connect them, and say what the available evidence does and does not support.

The questions are in a useful order, but they are not separate boxes. Scientific inquiry runs through the entire course. Chemistry makes later biochemistry easier to understand. Cell biology gives physiology something to build on. Physics helps explain processes such as pressure, flow, electrical signalling, and imaging. Later medical study draws on all of these levels at once.

By the end, you should be able to do more than recognize scientific terms. You should be able to:

- explain causes and consequences step by step;
- make a model—a deliberately simpler version of a situation—use it to make a prediction, and explain its limits;
- investigate a question with suitable measurements; and
- combine several subjects in an unfamiliar problem.

Those abilities are preparation for further study. They are not a medical qualification or a promise of admission.

## Start from what you already notice

:::{check}
:id: check-starting-point

Choose an ordinary question about a living system. It might concern movement, sleep, infection, inheritance, food, or a laboratory measurement. Without looking anything up, name two kinds of science that might help answer it. Write one question each subject could address.
:::

There is no score here and no expected science vocabulary. If the subject names are unfamiliar, borrow two of the five bold questions above. The useful first step is noticing that one question can be viewed at more than one level.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to tell the story of the course, make at least two of its connections, and picture the work you are preparing to do. You are not being tested on chemistry, biology, or physics today.
