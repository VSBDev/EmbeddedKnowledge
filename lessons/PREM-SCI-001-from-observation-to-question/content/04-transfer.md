# Carry the skill into a new setting

Here is a fictional, aggregated campus-health record. It is designed for this lesson and contains no real patient information.

In a 2025 summary, 76% of scheduled follow-up visits were completed by patients recorded as living less than 10 kilometres from a campus clinic. The completion figure was 58% for patients recorded as living 10 kilometres or farther away.

Your task is not to explain the difference. Turn the observation into one bounded empirical question.

Before continuing, write a question that names:

- the patients and clinic covered by the record;
- the distance contrast;
- the follow-up record;
- the time period; and
- wording that does not assume distance caused the difference.

One defensible question is:

> Among patients with scheduled follow-up visits at the campus clinic in 2025, how did the proportion of completed visits differ between those recorded as living less than 10 kilometres away and those recorded as living 10 kilometres or farther away?

This question could be answered from the stated record. It does not tell us why the proportions differ. Transport access, appointment type, work schedules, recording errors, and many other features could matter; deciding among them requires new questions and suitable designs.

Now transfer once more: write a second question that would help narrow one possible explanation without asking for anyone's identity or personal medical details. For example, you could ask about the same completion measure across public-transport access categories in an appropriately authorized, de-identified dataset. Keep the wording descriptive unless the design can support a causal claim.

## What to carry forward

From memory, complete this chain:

> **Observation → ___ → ___ → ___ → empirical question**

A useful reconstruction is:

> **Observation → case or contrast → observable record → boundary and open outcome → empirical question**

The next lesson will begin with a question like yours and ask what explanation and prediction could be tested. Later lessons will revisit the same question when you choose variables, measurements, and a study design.

## Mastery check

The assessment contains three untimed items. Attempt each before opening its answer and reasoning. A complete response earns all ten available points: two for separating observation from explanation, four for constructing a bounded question, and four for evaluating a question in a new setting. Retakes are unlimited. On a retry, use the alternate cases in the answer feedback rather than copying your first response.

## Accessibility and alternatives

Every task can be completed in prose, bullets, or a table. You may type, dictate into your own device, or use assistive technology; no audio upload is required. The lesson does not depend on color, an image, dragging, speed, physical apple preparation, or access to a laboratory. The apple and clinic cases are fully described in text. You never need to provide personal or medical information.

## Recovery route

If observation and explanation blur together, circle words that name an unseen cause, judgment, or mechanism. Move those words out of the observation. Keep only what was recorded, where, and when.

If a question remains vague, write four headings—**case or contrast, record, boundary, open outcome**—and fill one line under each before trying the sentence again.

If you have a meaningful question that evidence alone cannot settle, do not force it into scientific wording. State the empirical part separately from the ethical, practical, or value decision.

If causal wording keeps slipping in, begin with “How does ___ differ between ___?” or “What pattern links ___ and ___?” That produces a descriptive question. Later, after learning study design, you can judge whether stronger wording is justified.
