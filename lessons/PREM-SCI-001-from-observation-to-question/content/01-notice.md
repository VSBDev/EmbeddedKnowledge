# Notice before you explain

Picture two freshly cut apple slices on a plate. One was brushed with lemon juice; the other was left alone. Twenty minutes later, the plain slice looks browner.

That small difference can start a real investigation. But first, pause over these two sentences:

1. **After twenty minutes, the untreated surface was darker than the lemon-coated surface.**
2. **Lemon juice slowed the reaction that makes apples brown.**

Which sentence records what someone noticed, and which one tries to explain it?

Choose before reading on.

The first sentence is an **observation**: it records something that could be checked, including what was compared and when. The second is an **explanation**. It may be right, but the color difference alone does not establish the cause.

That separation matters everywhere in science. A shape seen through a microscope, a temperature reading, and a pattern in a health dataset can all be observations. The story you tell about why the pattern appeared is an inference or explanation. Science needs both, but it does not treat them as the same thing.

In this lesson, you will learn how to move from “I noticed something” to a question that evidence can answer. By the end, you should be able to:

- separate a record from the explanation attached to it;
- name what you would compare or examine and what you would record;
- set enough boundaries that another person can understand the question; and
- leave room for the evidence to surprise you.

You do not need prior laboratory experience. Careful everyday noticing is enough to begin.

This lesson stops at the question. The next lessons take up hypotheses, predictions, variables, controls, measurement quality, and study design. There is no single script called “the scientific method” that every field follows in the same order; today you are learning one move that appears across many kinds of science.
