# Build a question you could answer

“Why did the lemon stop the apple going bad?” sounds scientific, but it moves too fast. It assumes the lemon *stopped* something, calls browning “going bad,” and does not say what evidence would count as an answer.

A useful question builder has four parts:

1. **The case or contrast:** What are you examining or comparing?
2. **The record:** What could you observe or measure?
3. **The boundary:** In which sample, setting, and time window?
4. **An open outcome:** Could the record reasonably point in more than one direction?

The four parts are a drafting aid, not a magic sentence pattern. Some fields compare groups, some follow one system through time, and some use existing observations rather than changing anything. What they share is a question connected to possible evidence.

:::{worked-example}
:id: worked-example-apple-question
:label: From a brown apple to an answerable question

**Starting point.** The untreated apple surface looked darker than the lemon-coated surface after twenty minutes.

**What are we asking?** We are not yet asking for the chemical mechanism. We first want to know whether the visible difference holds under a stated comparison.

**Choose the contrast.** Compare slices coated with lemon juice with slices coated with water. Naming both sides is clearer than saying “with lemon” and leaving the alternative unstated.

**Choose the record.** Record the number of minutes each slice takes to reach level 3 on a supplied browning card that runs from 0 to 5. This turns “looks bad” into something people can record in the same form. Whether that card is a *good* measure is a later measurement question; here it makes the proposed record visible.

**Set the boundary.** Limit the question to freshly cut Granny Smith apple slices kept between 22 and 24 degrees Celsius during the first 60 minutes. This does not guarantee a fair study. It tells us what the question is and is not about.

**Write the question.** *For freshly cut Granny Smith apple slices kept at 22–24 °C, what is the difference in time to reach browning level 3 between lemon-juice-coated and water-coated slices during the first 60 minutes?*

**Check it.** We could record a longer time, the same time, a shorter time, or no slice reaching level 3. Each result would tell us something. The wording does not force the answer to favor lemon.

**Interpret the limit.** Answering this question would describe the comparison under these conditions. It would not, by itself, explain the chemistry, prove that the same result holds for every apple, or show that browned food is unsafe.

**Self-explanation.** Before reading on, name the one change that most improved the original question and explain why.
:::

A strong answer may choose the record: “going bad” became minutes to a stated browning level, so an observation can answer the question. Choosing the contrast and boundary also matters. If you only changed the grammar, try again and name how the possible evidence became clearer.

## Testable, measurable, and falsifiable

People sometimes call a question “falsifiable.” More precisely, a **question** is empirically answerable; a **hypothesis or claim** is what evidence can count against.

For example, “lemon-coated slices take longer to brown” is a claim. Equal or shorter times would count against it under the stated conditions. The question above stays open: it asks what the difference is.

Use a practical test before you keep a question:

- Can you name a record that would bear on the answer?
- Could you describe a result that differs from your expectation?
- Is the question narrow enough to investigate with the time, access, and tools available?
- Does the wording avoid smuggling in a cause, judgment, or desired result?

Not every meaningful question is an empirical science question. “Is it fair to use animals in this study?” is an ethical question. Evidence can inform it, but evidence alone does not decide the value judgment. “How should a clinic spend a limited budget?” also needs priorities, not only measurements. Calling those questions non-empirical does not make them unimportant.
