# Practise the turn from noticing to testing

First, retrieve the question builder without looking back. Write four short prompts for yourself.

:::{check}
:id: check-retrieve-builder
:kind: retrieval

What four choices turn a broad curiosity into a question connected to possible evidence? Commit your list, then continue.
:::

Compare your list with: **case or contrast; observable record; boundary; open outcome**. Equivalent wording is fine. If one is missing, add it and say what problem that part prevents.

## One part supplied

Observation: in the same kitchen, a spoonful of sugar seemed to disappear faster in warm water than in cold water.

Complete this question:

> For ___, what is the difference in ___ between ___ and ___ during ___?

One defensible completion is:

> For 5 grams of granulated sugar stirred at the same rate in 200 millilitres of water, what is the difference in seconds until no crystals are visible between water at 15 °C and water at 45 °C during a single mixing trial?

This version names a contrast, a record, and conditions. It is still only a question, not a complete study plan. If your answer said “Why does heat make sugar dissolve faster?”, remove the assumed direction and make the record visible.

## Less support

Observation: on three mornings after rain, the water in a campus pond looked cloudier than it did on three dry mornings.

Draft a question without using the words *better*, *worse*, or *because*. Then check whether your question identifies:

- the pond or sampling locations;
- rainy and dry periods, or one clearly defined time series;
- a record such as turbidity—a measure of how cloudy the water is—reported in the same units; and
- a time boundary.

A defensible example is: *At the north and south sampling points of Campus Pond during September, how does turbidity measured at 09:00 differ on mornings after at least 10 millimetres of rain and mornings after 48 rain-free hours?*

That question asks about a measured pattern. It does not yet establish that rainfall caused the difference.

:::{misconception}
:id: misconception-cause-makes-testable
:kind: boundary

**Commit first.** Is “Does music cause better health?” testable as written? Answer yes or no and give one reason.

**Tempting shortcut:** adding “does X cause Y?” makes any question scientific.

**Why the shortcut fails:** *music*, *better health*, the people or system, and the time period are all unspecified. The word *cause* also asks for a stronger conclusion than a simple pattern can provide.

**Repair:** first decide what can be recorded and in whom or what. A narrower question might ask, “Among adult volunteers in a specified study, how does self-reported anxiety on a named scale differ immediately after ten minutes of silence and ten minutes of instrumental music?” Whether the study can support a causal conclusion depends on its design; later lessons handle that decision.

**Recheck:** revise “Does screen time cause poor sleep?” into a bounded question about a recordable pattern without claiming that one factor causes the other.
:::

One possible recheck is: *Among first-year university students who choose to join a seven-day diary study, how is reported screen use during the hour before bed associated with sleep duration recorded that night?* This is answerable as an observational question. It does not show that screen use caused the sleep pattern, and it does not request private health data from you.

## Independent practice

Choose an observation from biology, chemistry, physics, or everyday life. Write one sentence that records the observation without explaining it. Then write an empirical question with all four parts. Finally, write one possible result that would surprise you.

If you get stuck, the final scene's **Recovery route** turns the four parts into four headings you can fill in before writing a full sentence.

Check your work:

- If the observation contains *because*, a hidden mechanism, or a judgment, rewrite it as a record.
- If two people could collect completely different kinds of evidence and both claim to have answered the question, name the record more clearly.
- If only your expected answer seems possible, remove the loaded wording.
- If the question tries to settle a mechanism, a population, and a treatment decision at once, narrow it to one answerable step.
