# A retrieved record is not a vetted answer

## Commit before reading the repair

A PubMed result appears first under Best Match. Which conclusion is warranted?

A. NLM judged it the highest-quality article.

B. The ranking system estimated that it was relevant to the query, but the article still requires appraisal.

C. It must be a systematic review.

Write A, B, or C and one reason before continuing.

:::{misconception}
:id: misconception-ranking-equals-quality

**The tempting model.** “First result” sounds like “best evidence,” so ranking position is treated as an endorsement or quality score.

**What that model cannot explain.** PubMed describes Best Match as a relevance-ranking process. Separately, NLM states that inclusion in PubMed, PubMed Central, or Bookshelf is not endorsement and that it does not judge the quality of each individual item after database selection.

**Repair.** A discovery system answers *Which records appear relevant to this query?* Appraisal asks *How trustworthy, applicable, current, and fit for purpose is this item?* Those questions require different evidence and different steps.

**Compare on the same case.** Option B is warranted. Option A confuses relevance ranking with appraisal. Option C assigns a document type that ranking position alone cannot establish.

**Recheck in a new case.** A guideline appears in Bookshelf. Does its presence prove that it applies in your jurisdiction today? No. Its document type may match a recommendation need, but issuer, scope, date, development methods, and authority still require checking.
:::

:::{source-note}
:claims: claim-source-function-match, claim-database-not-appraisal
:sources: source-pubmed-user-guide, source-bookshelf-overview, source-nlm-publication-types, source-pubmed-disclaimer

The PubMed guide describes relevance ranking, while the NLM disclaimer states the non-endorsement and individual-quality boundary. The other sources support the container-versus-document distinction used in the recheck.
:::
