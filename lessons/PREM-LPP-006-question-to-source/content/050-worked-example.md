# Work from a study need to a search prototype

This complete model uses a fictional learner and a constructed result sample. The sample is instructional, not a claim about a live PubMed search.

:::{worked-example}
:id: worked-example-question-to-source

**Task.** A learner needs a current synthesis addressing whether retrieval practice improves delayed recall among university biology learners. They must produce a defensible starting plan, not a final evidence conclusion.

**Represent the givens and unknowns.**

| Element | Representation |
| --- | --- |
| Use | Prepare a short study-method briefing |
| Known subject | Retrieval practice in university biology learning |
| Needed outcome | Delayed recall or retention |
| Unknown | Which sources synthesize the relevant evidence |
| Boundary | No personal clinical decision; no claim that retrieved items are high quality |

**Select the model.** The needed answer product combines findings across studies, so a systematic review is a plausible document type. PubMed is a plausible discovery environment for candidate biomedical and health-science records. This choice follows source function, not a universal source hierarchy.

**Plan.** Extract the minimum discriminating concepts, draft meaning-preserving variants, make a simple prototype, inspect its translation and a small sample, then revise one block.

**Execute.**

1. Remove the narrative purpose phrase “prepare a short briefing.” It affects use, not topical relevance.
2. Draft three blocks: `[university biology learners] [retrieval practice] [delayed recall]`.
3. Add candidates inside the blocks: `[university biology learners | undergraduate biology students] [retrieval practice | practice testing] [delayed recall | long-term retention]`.
4. Start with the simplest expression of one term from each block. Do not yet claim this is a comprehensive or reproducible query.
5. Inspect the database's translation. In the constructed rehearsal, all three ideas are represented, but the result sample contains few plausible syntheses and several relevant-looking titles use broader population wording such as “undergraduate students.”
6. Revise only the population block to test `[university learners | undergraduate students]`. Keep “biology” as a later eligibility check rather than an initial required term.

**Check the plan independently.** The answer product and source function now match. Every retained block can change relevance. The revision responds to an observed wording mismatch rather than random trial and error. The relaxed population block may improve recall but may also retrieve studies outside biology, so the learner records that trade-off. Because the question crosses biomedical and education literature, PubMed is only a prototype environment; a comprehensive later search would also require an education-focused database or an information specialist.

**Interpretation.** The revised plan can locate candidate reviews. It cannot establish that a review is current, methodologically sound, applicable to biology learners, or suitable for a recommendation. Those are appraisal questions.

**Self-explanation.** Which decision mattered most: choosing PubMed, naming the answer product, or adding a synonym? Explain why changing that decision would alter the route.
