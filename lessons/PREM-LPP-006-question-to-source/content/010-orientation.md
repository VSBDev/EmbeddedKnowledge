# A search box is not an answer box

Imagine that you need evidence for a laboratory discussion tomorrow. Typing the whole question into the first search box you see may return many plausible-looking items, yet still leave you unsure whether you found an overview, a study, a recommendation, or merely a list of citations.

This lesson gives you a decision chain for that earlier stage of research:

`knowledge need → answer product → starting source → concept blocks → revision signal`

By the end, you will be able to justify a starting source, turn a natural-language need into searchable concepts, and revise a first prototype without treating retrieval as evidence appraisal.

## Scope

You will work at a foundation level with short biomedical and education examples. You will not yet judge source authority, construct a comprehensive reproducible database strategy, read a paper critically, or synthesize findings. Those tasks belong to later outcomes. This lesson also does not provide personal medical advice or authorize a clinical decision.

:::{callout}
:kind: boundary

Every health-related case in this lesson is fictional and exists only to practise information seeking. Do not use a classroom search plan to diagnose, select treatment, or replace a qualified professional or information specialist.
:::

## Accessibility and alternatives

All required relationships appear in text and linear tables. You may draft concept blocks with a keyboard, on paper, or by dictating to a scribe. No task depends on color, position, sound, motion, dragging, a time limit, personal health information, or access to a paid database. In print, read each table from its header row downward.
