# Authoring brief: PREM-LPP-006

## Target and learner

- **Outcome:** `topic-study-research-literacy-question-to-source` (`PREM-02.01`), “Turn a knowledge need into searchable concepts and choose an appropriate source type.”
- **Declared learner:** a foundation-level Premed learner who can state what they want to know but has not been taught formal database-search construction.
- **Graph prerequisites:** none.
- **Useful prior idea:** the learner can distinguish a topic from a question in ordinary language. This is activated but not assumed as a mastered graph outcome.
- **Exclusions:** formal source-authority appraisal (`PREM-02.02`), reproducible database syntax and inclusion criteria (`PREM-02.03`), paper anatomy, citation practice, evidence synthesis, and personal clinical decision-making.

## Instructional argument

The central task is to stop treating a search box as an answer box. A learner first names the **answer product** needed, then selects a source type or discovery environment that can plausibly supply that product. The learner next removes narrative wrapping from the knowledge need, retains the concepts that determine relevance, and generates ordinary-language variants for each concept block. The first query is a diagnostic prototype: its translation and a small result sample show which concept block needs revision. Source selection and query revision locate candidates; neither establishes that a candidate is accurate or fit for a decision.

The model is:

1. state the knowledge need as a bounded question;
2. name the answer product needed now;
3. choose a starting source type or discovery environment whose function matches that product;
4. extract two to four discriminating concept blocks;
5. add variants within each block without adding a new concept;
6. run a prototype, inspect how it was interpreted and what it retrieves, then revise one block;
7. hand the candidates to the later appraisal workflow.

This is a starting heuristic, not a universal source hierarchy. Complex reviews, rare concepts, non-biomedical questions, and high-stakes decisions require broader databases and specialist help.

## Observable mastery evidence

The learner can:

- identify whether a knowledge need asks for orientation, a synthesis, original methods/results, a recommendation, or citation discovery, and justify a matching starting source type;
- convert a natural-language need into concept blocks with defensible synonyms or spelling variants;
- inspect a prototype search and make one reasoned revision without confusing retrieval with evidence appraisal.

Assessment items separately test source choice, concept extraction, the database-quality boundary, and integrated transfer.

## Diagnostic and recovery

The opening diagnostic asks learners to mark the words in a question that would have to be present in a relevant source, identify the desired answer product, and distinguish a document type from a search service. Learners who cannot do one of those tasks take the explicit recovery route: rewrite the need with “I need ___ in order to ___,” choose one answer-product card, and use the provided noun/relationship prompts before continuing.

## Worked example and practice progression

- **Complete model:** a fictional learner seeks a current synthesis about spaced retrieval and delayed recall in university biology learners. The example exposes the knowledge need, answer product, source choice, concept blocks, variants, prototype, result-sample check, revision, and scope boundary.
- **Completion problem:** the answer product and two concept blocks are supplied; the learner supplies the missing block and source choice.
- **Supported problem:** prompts name the needed decisions but not their content.
- **Independent problem:** the learner constructs blocks and a source choice from a new knowledge need.
- **Mixed selection:** cases alternate among reference work, evidence synthesis, primary research, guideline, and bibliographic database.
- **Transfer:** a non-clinical public-health planning question requires the learner to coordinate source purpose, jurisdiction/date, concepts, and a revision rule.

## Misconception and boundary

The high-value error is: “If a result appears near the top of PubMed, it is the best answer.” The repair distinguishes discovery from appraisal: a bibliographic database indexes and retrieves records but does not endorse each item or judge each article’s quality. A second boundary distinguishes a database from the documents it helps locate.

## Representations

- A repeated text table maps **knowledge need → answer product → starting source → concept blocks → revision signal**. Its job is to make each decision and its dependency explicit.
- Bracketed concept blocks provide a linear, nonvisual representation that survives reflow, print, and assistive technology.
- No image or diagram is necessary; spatial or color coding would add no instructional value.

## Retrieval, transfer, and later spacing

A retrieval scene asks learners to reconstruct the five decisions before feedback. The synthesis schedules a later no-notes retrieval before `PREM-02.03`, where the learner will turn the current concept plan into a documented, reproducible search. `PREM-02.02` will revisit source labels while adding authority and hierarchy judgments.

## Feedback and remediation

Feedback always identifies the decision that failed: answer-product mismatch, an omitted concept, a synonym that changes meaning, overconstraint, or a retrieval/appraisal confusion. The next action is correspondingly to re-state the need, change the starting source type, repair one block, remove one constraint, or defer quality judgment to appraisal. Retakes use changed contexts rather than the same wording.

## Accessibility, safety, and route boundaries

All essential relationships are expressed in prose and linear tables. No task requires color, sound, motion, dragging, timed performance, personal health data, or access to a paid database. Typed, spoken-to-a-scribe, and paper responses are equivalent. Clinical-flavored prompts are fictional information-literacy tasks and do not ask learners to diagnose, recommend care, or search for personal treatment advice.
