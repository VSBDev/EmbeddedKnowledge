# Test an outside assignment rule

A fictional regional program offers classroom air-filtration grants to municipalities with an enrollment count just above a cutoff fixed two years earlier. The research team did not create the cutoff or choose recipients. It can obtain authorized, aggregated records of classroom particle concentration before and after installation for municipalities close to either side of the cutoff.

Do not treat the words *program* or *cutoff* as the answer. Build the design from the assignment process.

Before reading on, write:

1. the exposure and outcome;
2. who assigned exposure;
3. why municipalities just above and below the cutoff might form a useful comparison;
4. two facts that would weaken that comparison;
5. the design label you would use now; and
6. the strongest conclusion you would permit if the checks were satisfactory.

## A defensible analysis

The exposure is receiving the filtration grant; the outcome is the defined change in classroom particle concentration. The program, not the research team, assigned exposure according to an earlier cutoff. Municipalities near either side may be similar enough for the cutoff to create a **natural-experiment candidate**.

That candidate becomes less persuasive if municipalities could alter their reported enrollment to cross the cutoff, if another program used the same cutoff, if records were collected differently across groups, or if eligibility exceptions tracked expected particle changes. The research team must examine the actual assignment and implementation rather than assuming “above” and “below” are interchangeable.

If the cutoff was fixed, not manipulable, applied consistently, and the nearby groups were otherwise comparable on relevant pre-exposure features, a difference in outcome change could support a bounded causal interpretation of grant assignment near that cutoff. That conclusion would still depend on the stated assumptions and would not automatically generalize to municipalities far from the boundary.

If the assignment process is not defensible, keep the useful part of the question and change the claim. The same records could support an observational cohort comparison of grant exposure followed by later particle outcomes, with association—not causation—as the default interpretation.

This is genuine design choice: the data file may stay the same while the warranted inference changes when the assignment facts change.

> observation → bounded empirical question → hypothesis → conditions → prediction → variable-and-control audit → ___

The missing link is **choose a study design that makes the needed comparison possible**.

- In this map, the question slot names what needs answering.
- The hypothesis, conditions, and prediction slots record a proposal and the observations expected under its stated conditions.
- The variable-and-control audit slot makes the intended comparison and rival factors visible.
- The study-design slot asks who creates the comparison, where it enters time, and how strongly it can support an inference.

The next outcomes examine sampling, randomization, and blinding; measurement quality; correlation and causation; and specific biases. This lesson does not replace those checks. It gives you the first choice: experiment, cohort, case-control, cross-sectional, or natural experiment.

## Recovery route

If all five labels blur together, ignore the names and answer two ordinary questions: **Who created the exposure difference? Did researchers start with exposure, start with outcome, or take one snapshot?** Then attach the label.

If prospective and retrospective cohorts split apart, draw exposure before outcome for both. Add “researchers collect forward” to the prospective version and “researchers assemble existing records” to the retrospective version.

If cohort and case-control reverse, name the entry group. Exposure or a source population first means cohort; cases and controls first means case-control.

If cross-sectional seems causal, ask whether the snapshot shows which variable came first. If not, keep the conclusion descriptive or associative.

If every policy looks like a natural experiment, write the assignment rule and two rival reasons for exposure. Without a defensible outside process, use the ordinary observational label and narrow the inference.

If random assignment feels like a guarantee, check whether assignment was implemented, outcomes were measured comparably, and follow-up preserved the intended comparison. Later lessons make those checks more precise.
