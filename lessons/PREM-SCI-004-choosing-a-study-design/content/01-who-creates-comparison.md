# Who creates the comparison?

Two campus residence halls report different morning-alertness scores. Hall A introduced quiet hours last month; Hall B did not. A dean asks, “Did quiet hours improve alertness?”

You cannot answer honestly from the score difference alone. Students may have chosen Hall A because they already preferred quiet evenings. The halls may differ in class schedules or distance from late-night venues. If researchers instead assigned eligible volunteers by chance to the two policies and measured them in the same way, the same score difference could support a stronger claim.

The design changes what the comparison means.

In this lesson, you will learn to choose among five ways of making a comparison: an experiment, a cohort study, a case-control study, a cross-sectional study, and a natural experiment. The useful question is not “Which label sounds most scientific?” It is “Who created the exposure difference? Did researchers start with exposure, start with outcome, or take one snapshot? What conclusion can that comparison support?”

Study design is the next decision: it determines which comparison can actually be made.

## First decision: who creates the exposure difference?

The possible cause or condition of interest is the **exposure**. In the residence case, exposure means living under the quiet-hours policy. The record to be compared is the **outcome**. Here, the outcome is a morning-alertness score measured in a stated way.

If the research team deliberately assigns the exposure, the study is an **experiment**. The team might recruit eligible volunteers and assign each to a quiet-hours or usual-hours residence. **Random assignment** gives each eligible participant a known chance of entering either condition. It makes the groups comparable in expectation, so a well-implemented randomized experiment can support a stronger causal inference than a comparison of self-selected halls. Chance imbalances, departures from the assigned policy, missing records, or unequal measurement can still weaken the inference.

If the research team does not assign exposure, the study is **observational**. Researchers might follow people who already chose different halls, examine earlier records, or survey students where they live now. An observed association alone does not establish that quiet hours caused the outcome.

A **natural experiment** uses an exposure difference created by an external event, policy, rule, or implementation process rather than by the research team. Suppose the housing office introduced quiet hours according to a schedule fixed before any alertness scores were known. That creates a possible natural experiment, but the label is not enough. Researchers must explain who received the policy and why. If the schedule also tracks building location or resident preferences, those differences remain rival explanations.

## Second decision: where do you enter the timeline?

When exposure was not assigned for the study, imagine a line from exposure to later outcome. The starting point separates three common observational designs.

- A **cohort study** begins with a defined population or exposure groups and compares subsequent outcomes. A prospective cohort collects exposure information and follows forward. A retrospective cohort assembles existing records in which exposure was documented before the later outcome.
- A **case-control study** begins by selecting **cases**, which have the outcome, and comparison people called **controls**, which do not. These controls are not the positive- or negative-control conditions used to check an experiment. Investigators reconstruct earlier exposure in the same way for both groups, and the controls should represent the population that produced the cases. Starting with the outcome can be practical when that outcome is uncommon.
- A **cross-sectional study** measures exposure and outcome in a defined population at one time or over one brief survey window. It can estimate **prevalence**—the proportion with a condition or characteristic at that time—but usually cannot show which variable came first.

| Design | Who creates or selects the comparison? | Central time logic | A best-fit question |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Experiment | Researcher assigns an intervention or exposure | assignment → later outcome | What is the effect of an ethically assignable intervention? |
| Cohort | Researcher defines a population or exposure groups | exposure → subsequent outcome | What outcomes develop after a recorded exposure? |
| Case-control | Researcher selects cases and controls by outcome | outcome → earlier exposure | Which prior exposures differ for an uncommon outcome? |
| Cross-sectional | Researcher samples a population at one time | exposure and outcome measured together | How common is a characteristic now, and what is it associated with? |
| Natural experiment | An outside event or rule creates exposure groups | external assignment → outcome | What changed across a defensible outside assignment process? |

Read the table row by row and you have the whole comparison; the layout adds nothing extra.
