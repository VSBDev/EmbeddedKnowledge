# Make two explanations disagree

Return to the apple question. The case and numerical results below are fictional. You do not need apples, laboratory access, or personal experience with browning to follow the reasoning.

:::{worked-example}
:id: worked-example-apple-comparison

**1. Start with the question.**

> For freshly cut Granny Smith apple slices kept at 22–24 °C, what is the difference in time to reach browning level 3 between lemon-juice-coated and water-coated slices during the first 60 minutes?

**2. State competing hypotheses.**

- **Hypothesis A:** A dissolved substance specific to lemon juice slows browning under these conditions.
- **Hypothesis B:** Changing the coating from water to lemon juice does not change the browning time under these conditions.

These are proposals for the exercise, not established claims about apple chemistry.

**3. Hold the relevant conditions in view.**

Both hypotheses refer to freshly cut Granny Smith slices, the two stated coatings, 22–24 °C, and the first 60 minutes. If those conditions change, derive again instead of carrying the prediction over automatically.

**4. Derive each expected record.**

| If this hypothesis holds under the stated conditions… | …then this record is expected |
| --- | --- |
| A: a lemon-specific dissolved substance slows browning | Lemon-coated slices take longer than water-coated slices to reach level 3 |
| B: changing between these coatings does not change browning time | Lemon-coated and water-coated slices reach level 3 at about the same time |

The time difference is a **discriminating prediction** because the two hypotheses expect different results. Merely predicting “the slices will brown” would not separate them.

**5. Compare with the supplied fictional result.**

Suppose the summary record reports a median (the middle recorded time) of 41 minutes for the lemon-coated slices and 23 minutes for the water-coated slices.

That direction agrees with A's prediction and conflicts with B's equal-time prediction. In this comparison, the record therefore supports A over B.

**6. Stop where the evidence stops.**

The result does not prove that a particular lemon substance caused the difference. Other explanations may make the same prediction, and the result's strength depends on how samples, measurements, and conditions were handled. The careful conclusion is:

> Under the stated fictional conditions, the result is consistent with Hypothesis A and counts against Hypothesis B as written. It does not by itself establish the mechanism or eliminate untested explanations.
:::

Notice the decisions that make the comparison useful:

- The hypotheses answer the same question.
- Each prediction names the observation, not only a vague direction such as “it will work.”
- Conditions appear before the arrow.
- The expected records disagree.
- The conclusion compares support without turning agreement into proof.

Now alter the fictional result: both groups reach level 3 at a median of 27 minutes. Before reading on, decide which hypothesis that result counts against and which it supports.

The equal-time result counts against A as written and agrees with B's prediction. It would not prove B. A third hypothesis might also expect equal times.

This is why contrary results are productive. They tell you which part of an explanation may need rejection, narrowing, or revision.
