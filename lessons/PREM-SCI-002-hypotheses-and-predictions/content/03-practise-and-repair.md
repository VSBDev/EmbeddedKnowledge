# Practise the reasoning and repair shortcuts

Begin with retrieval. Without looking back, write one sentence for each job:

- an empirical question;
- a hypothesis; and
- a prediction.

Then draw the arrow **hypothesis + conditions → expected observation**. If the sentence after the arrow explains *why*, it is probably another hypothesis rather than a prediction.

## One part supplied

A fictional mystery box beeped when a red magnetic token was inserted.

- Question: Which observable property of a token is associated with the beep?
- Hypothesis: The box beeps when the inserted token is magnetic. Treat this as the complete explanation of when the box beeps.

Complete the prediction for a blue magnetic token and a red nonmagnetic token. Commit your answer before continuing.

One defensible prediction is: **if magnetism triggers the beep under the same box settings, then the blue magnetic token will produce a beep and the red nonmagnetic token will not.** Token color is changed independently of magnetism, so this expected pattern separates the proposed property from color.

A result that counts against the hypothesis as written would be a blue magnetic token that does not produce a beep while a red nonmagnetic token does under the same settings.

## Make two hypotheses disagree

A fictional greenhouse alarm sounds unexpectedly. Two explanations are proposed:

For this simplified comparison, treat each hypothesis as the complete explanation of when the alarm sounds:

- **Hypothesis T:** the alarm sounds when the sensor temperature is above its threshold and otherwise remains silent.
- **Hypothesis L:** the alarm sounds when stray light reaches the sensor and otherwise remains silent.

Copy the table and fill every blank before reading the feedback.

| Condition | Prediction from Hypothesis T | Prediction from Hypothesis L |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Bright but below the temperature threshold | ___ | ___ |
| Dark but above the temperature threshold | ___ | ___ |

The discriminating expectations are:

| Condition | Prediction from Hypothesis T | Prediction from Hypothesis L |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Bright but below the temperature threshold | Alarm does not sound | Alarm sounds |
| Dark but above the temperature threshold | Alarm sounds | Alarm does not sound |

If you first thought both mechanisms could operate, that is sensible caution about a real device. This exercise instead tests each simplified hypothesis as the complete explanation, so return to that stated assumption and ask what each one commits you to under the two conditions.

## Less support

A covered cup ended a classroom demonstration warmer than an uncovered cup. Treat the observation as fictional; do not perform the demonstration.

Write:

1. one hypothesis about reduced energy transfer to the surroundings;
2. a different hypothesis about the two cups having started at different temperatures;
3. a prediction from each under one clearly named comparison; and
4. the result on which the two predictions differ.

A defensible comparison would begin both cups at the same recorded temperature, use otherwise matched cups, and compare later temperatures. The reduced-transfer hypothesis predicts that the covered cup will end warmer. The different-starting-temperature hypothesis, as the complete explanation, predicts no consistent covered-versus-uncovered difference once the starting temperatures are matched. The later temperature difference is therefore discriminating for these two simplified hypotheses.

Your wording can differ. Check that each prediction follows from its own hypothesis and named conditions rather than from the answer you prefer.

:::{misconception}
:id: misconception-matching-proves
:kind: boundary

**Commit first.** A prediction matches a result. Does that prove the hypothesis? Answer yes or no and give one reason.

**Tempting shortcut:** “My prediction was right, so my explanation is true.”

**Why the shortcut fails:** more than one explanation can lead to the same expected record. Measurement problems or unexamined conditions may also matter. A match supports the hypothesis in the comparison made; it does not automatically establish a cause.

**Repair:** name the competitors and use calibrated language: *consistent with*, *supports relative to*, or *counts against*. Then ask what different record the remaining hypotheses predict.

**Recheck:** Two hypotheses both predict that a sealed jar's mass will remain unchanged over ten minutes. The mass remains unchanged. What can you conclude? A careful answer says the result agrees with both predictions and therefore does not discriminate between those hypotheses.
:::

## Independent practice

Choose an ordinary observation that does not require you to collect new data. Write two plausible hypotheses, state the conditions, and derive one expected record from each. Highlight the place where the predictions differ. Finally, name a possible result that counts against each hypothesis.

If both hypotheses always predict the same thing, either revise the comparison or add a third condition that makes their commitments visible. Do not add complexity merely to sound scientific; add only what changes the expected observation.
