# From what you want to know to what you expect to see

Imagine that you have already asked this bounded question:

> For freshly cut Granny Smith apple slices kept at 22–24 °C, what is the difference in time to reach browning level 3 between lemon-juice-coated and water-coated slices during the first 60 minutes?

The question tells you what you want to find out. It does not yet say why the coatings might differ or what record you expect.

If the data arrived today, how could you tell whether they challenge an explanation rather than merely decorate a story made afterward? You need two more links: a proposed answer and the observable result that answer commits you to.

Before reading on, label each sentence **question**, **hypothesis**, or **prediction**:

1. A dissolved substance specific to lemon juice slows browning under these conditions.
2. Will lemon-juice-coated and water-coated slices take different times to reach browning level 3?
3. If the lemon-specific explanation is correct under the stated conditions, the lemon-coated slices will take longer to reach level 3 than the water-coated slices.

The order is **hypothesis, question, prediction**. The three sentences do different jobs.

| Part of the reasoning | Its job | Useful form |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Empirical question | Names what evidence should help answer | What difference or pattern is present? |
| Hypothesis | Proposes an explanation or relationship | The difference occurs because… |
| Prediction | States the record expected if the hypothesis holds under named conditions | If the hypothesis and conditions hold, then we expect to observe… |

A hypothesis is not merely a topic or a guess. It is a proposed explanation or relationship that can meet evidence. A useful hypothesis must risk being wrong: ask what result would count against it.

A prediction is not the same as saying what you hope will happen. It is a logical bridge from the hypothesis to an observable record:

> **hypothesis + stated conditions → expected observation**

The conditions matter. A hypothesis might lead to one expectation at room temperature and another outside that range. This lesson names conditions so you can see the reasoning; deciding how to control and measure them comes next.

Write the prediction before you inspect the relevant result. That keeps you from quietly changing the expected result to match what you have already seen. Some fields use *prediction* mainly for a novel or future observation. In this lesson, an archived but still unseen record can also test the logic, provided you lock the prediction before opening that record.

## One question can have competing answers

Suppose a second student proposes:

> Changing the coating from water to lemon juice does not change the browning time under these conditions.

That is a different hypothesis. It leads to a different expectation: lemon-coated and water-coated slices should reach level 3 at about the same time. Now the same record can separate two explanations because the explanations do not expect the same outcome.

The strongest comparison is not “Did my preferred result occur?” It is “Which result should occur under each plausible hypothesis, and where do their expectations differ?”

:::{check}
:id: check-role-retrieval
:kind: retrieval

Without looking back, complete the two blanks so the chain has three distinct parts:

> What we want to find out → ___ → ___

Then add the observation that would count against your hypothesis.
:::

A useful reconstruction is: **empirical question → proposed explanation or relationship → expected observation under stated conditions**. If you cannot name a contrary observation, make either the hypothesis, conditions, or record more specific.
